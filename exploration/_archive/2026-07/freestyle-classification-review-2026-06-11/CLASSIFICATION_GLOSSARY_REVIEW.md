# Freestyle Classification & Glossary Review

Read-only review. **Proposes glossary additions only — no DB, schema, `trick_family`, or doctrine
changes.** Every proposal lands (if approved) as glossary prose or a reversible content overlay, in
the pedagogy layer, never as a canonical-family reclassification.

## Spine: preserve the core, document the edges

The core doctrine holds: **most tricks are recognized movement patterns that terminate in a
controlled contact (stall or kick).** Nothing below weakens that. The edge cases are handled as
**named exception classes**, **observational groupings**, or **educational decompositions** — three
distinct registers, each kept in its lane by the existing `≈` (intuition) vs `≡` (structural
equivalence) discipline.

Two cross-cutting rules drive every call:
- **Enumerate only what exists.** Several proposed members are not canonical rows (below); the
  glossary must not imply they are.
- **Terminal contact is part of identity.** A kick form is *related to*, not *identical to*, its
  stall form. "Same topology, different terminator" = a relationship, not a merge.

## A. Swing-element exception class — RECOMMEND (formalize what's already half-present)

**Structural analysis.** Pendulum (`TOE SWING (SET) > SAME TOE [DEL]`, 2 ADD) and Rake
(`SET > SWING [DEX] > SAME TOE [DEL]`, 2 ADD) are curator-locked under a swing-element doctrine
(swing = a +1 structural movement element, not a named operator). The glossary **already** documents
them: *"Pendulum and Rake show how the same elements can name two distinct primitives … swing then
toe vs toe then swing."* But that note sits inside the Ben Job "finite primitives" essay; it is not
framed as an **exception to the terminate-in-contact rule**.

**Recommendation.** Add one short paragraph to **§core-concepts**, beside the trick definition:
> Most tricks terminate in a controlled contact (a stall or a kick). A small number of recognized
> tricks terminate through a controlled **swing element** instead — the swing motion itself completes
> the trick, which may then settle into a stall, a kick, a catch, or a follow-on move. Pendulum and
> Rake are the primary examples.

Cross-link to the existing primitives note. **Do not expand the core definition** — keep it as a
named exception class. **Doctrine risk: LOW** (doctrine already curator-locked).

## B. Kick variants — RECOMMEND a CONCEPT note, NOT an enumerated family (CAUTION)

**Structural analysis.** The premise ("only the terminal contact differs") is pedagogically true, but
the enumerated examples mostly **do not exist as canonical rows**: of Mirage Kick / Illusion Kick /
Butterfly Kick / Pixie Kick / Fairy Kick, only **`butterfly-kick`** exists (plus `miraging-kick`).
`mirage-kick`, `illusion-kick`, `pixie-kick`, `fairy-kick` are not in the dictionary.

**Recommendation.** Add a **concept** note (§dexterities / terminal-contact), framed generically:
> Many stall-terminating topologies also have a **kick form**: the entry + dexterity skeleton is
> unchanged; only the terminal contact differs (a delay becomes a no-stall kick). Recognized pairs in
> this dictionary include Butterfly / Butterfly Kick and Miraging / Miraging Kick.

On trick pages, surface a **terminal-variant** relationship **only where both rows exist** — a
reversible overlay in the relationship-group model (the same mechanism as Movement neighbours /
Quantity ladder), **not** a `trick_family` change. **Doctrine risk: MEDIUM** — do not list
non-existent kick variants as if canonical, and frame the pair as *related* (shared skeleton), not
*identical* (terminal contact is part of identity).

## C. Clipper terminology — RECOMMEND an etymology note (LOW risk, high value)

**Structural analysis.** "Clipper" is genuinely overloaded across distinct rows: `clipper-stall`
(the inside cross-body **surface**), `clipper` (the base kick, 1 ADD), `flying-clipper`
(family=clipper, base=clipper), `toe-clipper` and `knee-clipper` (each its own family, 2 ADD).

**Recommendation.** Add a terminology note to **§surfaces**:
> "Clipper" names several things. The **clipper stall** is the inside cross-body catch surface. The
> **clipper** base trick kicks into that position. **Flying Clipper** is a flying (no-stall) clipper
> kick. **Toe Clipper** and **Knee Clipper** inherit the *clipper naming lineage* — the cross-body,
> tucked-behind-the-support-leg shape — even though the contact is the toe or knee, not a literal
> clipper stall.

Pure terminology/etymology; asserts no new structure. **Doctrine risk: LOW.**

## D. Continuous-control inside-delay family — RECOMMEND a glossary family note, with one reconciliation

**Structural analysis.** All four exist, and the family is **already partly documented**: wrap's own
description reads *"Held-delay leg-over family sibling to hop-over (jump variant) and walk-over (step
variant)."* Notation confirms the shared shape (inside delay → resolve to clipper):
- walk-over `SET > SAME INSIDE [DEL] > (plant foot) > OP OUT [DEX] > (into clipper)` (2)
- wrap `SET > SAME INSIDE [DEL] > OP OUT [DEX] > (into clipper)` (2)
- hop-over — jump variant (2)
- eclipse `SET > JUMP [BOD] > SAME/OP INSIDE [DEL] > OP OUT [DEX] > (land)` (3)

**Reconciliation required:** walk-over and wrap are **structurally near-identical** (the only delta is
the `(plant foot)` annotation — step vs pull naming of the same motion); a prior ruling treats
`walk-over ≡ wrap`. So the proposed 4-step progression conflates one structural node (walk-over / wrap)
with the genuinely distinct rungs. The defensible progression is **by body-movement complexity**:

> walk-over / wrap (step or pull, 2 ADD) → hop-over (jump, 2 ADD) → eclipse (jump + extra dex, 3 ADD)

**Recommendation.** Add a **§families** note grouping them as a continuous-control / held-delay
inside-delay neighborhood, distinguished by the body movement around the support leg, with walk-over
and wrap presented as **one node, two folk names**. They sit in separate `trick_family` slugs, so this
is an **observational glossary grouping, not a canonical family**. **Pedagogically useful + structurally
defensible. Doctrine risk: LOW-MEDIUM** (must not present as a `trick_family`; must honor walk-over ≡
wrap).

## E. Swirl-derived family note — RECOMMEND (short, descriptive, LOW risk)

**Structural analysis.** Confirmed: swirl `CLIP > SAME BACK SWIRL [DEX] > SAME CLIP [XBD] [DEL]`
**resolves to a clipper delay**; the family expands through structural variants (`whirling-swirl` =
whirl-modified swirl; `rev-swirl` = reverse), **not** kick forms; there is **no `swirl-kick` row**.

**Recommendation.** Add a short **§families** note (swirl family):
> Swirl resolves naturally to a clipper delay. Unlike most dexterity families, it has no recognized
> kick variant; the family grows through structural variants — whirling swirl, reverse swirling —
> rather than kick forms.

Descriptive and accurate. **Doctrine risk: LOW.**

## F. Twirl ≈ Swirl + Spin — RECOMMEND as an EDUCATIONAL decomposition, with the osis caveat

**Structural analysis.** This is the subtle one. Twirl's canonical reading is **reverse swirling
osis** (`trick_family = osis`, description: *"Folk name; decomposes to reverse swirling osis"*). But
its notation —
`CLIP >> SAME FRONT SWIRL [DEX] > (back) SPIN [BOD] > OP CLIP [XBD] [DEL]` —
**literally contains a swirl dex and a spin body element.** So "Twirl = Swirl + Spin" is *structurally
supportable as movement intuition*: the learner sees the two ingredients in the motion. It is **not**
the canonical structural label (which is reverse-swirling-osis).

**Recommendation.** Add it to **§composition**, in the educational-decomposition set beside the
existing ones, using `≈` (intuition), **never `≡`**, and naming the canonical reading alongside:
> Twirl ≈ Swirl + Spin (movement intuition). Canonical structural reading: reverse swirling osis.

This matches the established pattern: **Ripwalk ≈ Stepping Butterfly** (already carried as an IFPA
formula) and **Blur ≈ Stepping Paradox Mirage** (already in the derivation pilot, explicitly "folk
shorthand for the stepping + paradox stack"). **Structurally accurate enough for glossary use; improves
teaching/progression. Doctrine risk: LOW-MEDIUM** — the `≈`/`≡` discipline is the entire mitigation;
it must not displace the canonical osis reading.

## Consolidated doctrine risks

1. **Hardening observational groupings into canonical families** (D, B). → Glossary-only; never write
   `trick_family`.
2. **Implying non-existent tricks exist** (B: mirage/illusion/pixie/fairy kicks). → Enumerate only
   active rows.
3. **Collapsing `≈` into `≡`** (F, and the existing ripwalk/blur). → Rigid `≈` = intuition, `≡` =
   structural equivalence.
4. **Expanding the core trick definition** (A). → Document as a named *exception*, do not redefine.
5. **The walk-over ≡ wrap contradiction** (D). → One node, two folk names; not two progression steps.

## Educational relationships vs formal classifications

- **Formal (documented exception / factual):** A swing-element exception class; C clipper terminology;
  E swirl-derived note.
- **Observational grouping (glossary family, not `trick_family`):** D continuous-control inside-delay
  family.
- **Educational only (`≈`, never canonical):** F Twirl ≈ Swirl + Spin; B's "same skeleton, different
  terminator" framing (since most kick variants are not canonical rows). These join the existing
  Ripwalk ≈ Stepping Butterfly and Blur ≈ Stepping Paradox Mirage as movement-intuition aids, not
  structural claims.

Nothing here requires a schema change, a `trick_family` write, or a new canonical row. The only
trick-page surface affected is B's terminal-variant relationship, which reuses the existing reversible
relationship-group overlay and fires only where both stall and kick rows already exist.
