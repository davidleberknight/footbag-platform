## How to read this primer

This is a primer, not a dictionary. It reads top-to-bottom in one sitting. Concepts build on the concepts before them. The notation you'll meet here — `[set] > in dex > op toe` — is the symbolic shorthand the rest of the site uses; it's introduced as we go, never assumed.

If you want to look up a single word, jump to the **Traditional Glossary Reference** at the end. If you want to learn freestyle as a movement language, start here.

## §1.1 Freestyle is a language

Freestyle footbag looks improvisational, and at the level of a routine it is. But the moves themselves — the named tricks the community calls Mirage, Butterfly, Whirl, Phoenix — aren't a list of memorized recipes. They're sentences in a small, expressive language built from a handful of body actions and a clock that organizes them.

Once you can read the language, two things happen at once. First, individual trick names stop feeling like opaque vocabulary; you start hearing the structure inside them. Second, the way one trick relates to another stops feeling arbitrary; a Ripwalk is a Stepping Butterfly, a Mobius is a Spinning Torque, a Phoenix is a Pixie Ducking Butterfly. The names *are* their compositions.

This primer teaches the language in the order a learner can build it: the skeleton every trick shares, the body's vocabulary of surfaces and motions, the two dexterity styles you'll feel in your hip and your knee, the timing clock that organizes a single trick, and the way modifiers transform what's already there. By the end, you'll be reading tricks instead of memorizing them.

---

## §1.2 The skeleton every trick shares

Every freestyle trick — the simplest stall, the densest seven-add compound — fits the same five-beat skeleton:

```
1. contact     2. uptime         3. midtime        4. downtime       5. catch
   ─────────      ──────────        ──────────        ──────────        ─────────
   the bag       the bag goes      the bag           the bag           a part of
   meets a       up; something     is at its         comes down;       the body
   surface of    sets it           peak              something         meets the
   the body      moving                              shapes its        bag again
                                                     descent
```

A simple toe stall fills three of those beats and leaves the middle two empty: contact (the bag meets the toe), the support leg's small hop and the catch. A Mirage fills all five: the bag is set, the kicking leg circles inward during the descent, and the bag lands on the opposite toe.

A Phoenix fills all five and stacks two extra movements into the midtime: the head dips between the two dexes (a duck), and the set treatment up front compresses the uptime. The five beats are still there. There's just more happening inside them.

**This is the first thing to internalize.** You're not memorizing dozens of unrelated tricks. You're learning what fills each of five beats, and how to read a trick name as a description of *what fills which beat*.

## §1.3 Contact surfaces — where the body meets the bag

Before motion, the body and the bag have to meet. The surfaces involved are a small, learnable set:

- **The toe.** The top of the shoe; the most common stalling surface in freestyle. A toe stall is the floor of the entire vocabulary.
- **The clipper.** The inside surface of the shoe — but only when one leg is positioned in front of the other (a crossbody position). A clipper *is* an inside stall in a crossbody position. That positional requirement is the whole reason it has its own name.
- **The inside.** The same surface, but with the legs in a normal stance.
- **Less common surfaces:** outside (the outer edge of the shoe), heel, sole, knee, calf, back, head, shoulder. Each one has its own named contact when it shows up in tricks.

The two important surfaces for the rest of the primer are **toe** and **clipper**. Most tricks end on one or the other. Tricks that primarily live on the toe surface are *toe tricks* (Mirage, Legover, Pickup). Tricks that primarily live on the clipper surface are *clipper tricks* (Whirl, Butterfly, Osis).

This division shapes a lot of the language. Side defaults differ between them: when someone says "Toe Mirage" without specifying a side, they mean the *opposite-side* Mirage. When someone says "Clipper Whirl," they mean the *same-side* Whirl. The defaults are baked in, which is why the notation can drop them and still be read.

## §1.4 Sides — same-side and opposite-side

When the bag passes from one body part to another, it can land on the same vertical half of the body or the opposite vertical half. Freestyle has a small word for each:

- **ss** (same-side, sometimes written *near*): the next contact is on the same vertical half as the previous one.
- **op** (opposite-side, sometimes written *far*): the next contact is on the opposite vertical half.

Treat your body as two vertical strips, separated by a line down your sternum. A trick that goes from a right-foot set to a right-leg dex is ss. A trick that goes from a right-foot set to a left-leg dex is op. Sides are *relative*, not absolute — they describe the relationship between two consecutive components, not "this side of my body."

This matters because every dex in the language has a side. A Toe Mirage is a *toe op* — the set is on one foot, the dex returns to the toe on the other. A Clipper Whirl is a *clipper ss* — the clipper set and the clipper landing are both on the same side. The side flag is part of how a trick is identified.

The good news: the defaults usually do the work. Toe tricks default op, clipper tricks default ss. When you read a trick name without an explicit `ss` or `op` flag, the default applies. The flag only appears when the trick breaks the default — like an `ss Mirage`, which is a Mirage that defies its op default.

## §1.5 The dexterity — and the two ways your body does it

A **dexterity**, or **dex** for short, is the central freestyle primitive. It's the moment the kicking leg circles the bag while it's in the air. A toe set sends the bag up. The leg returns, traces a circle around the bag while it's mid-flight, and ends in position for the next contact.

Every trick that isn't a pure stall contains at least one dex. Most tricks contain more than one. The dexes are where the body does the work the trick is named for.

Dexes come in **two body styles**, and learning to feel the difference between them is the single most useful biomechanical distinction you can make as a freestyler.

### Hippy dex

In a **hippy dex**, the motion lives in the hip. The hip opens out, the thigh swings around the bag's path, and the calf trails along for the ride. The motion is *broad* — the leg moves as one piece, pivoting from the hip socket. The thigh is what circles the bag; the lower leg is along for the journey.

Tricks with hippy dexes tend to feel *opened* — the chest faces forward, the supporting hip and the kicking hip both rotate, and the trick reads as a swing of the whole leg.

**The canonical hippy-dex tricks are Mirage and Butterfly.** A Mirage is a hippy *in*-direction dex. A Butterfly is a hippy *out*-direction dex. The body is doing the same broad swing in both; what differs is which direction the leg travels.

### Leggy dex

In a **leggy dex**, the motion lives in the knee. The thigh stays relatively still; the lower leg pivots from the knee joint, and the calf — sometimes including the shin — is what traces the circle around the bag. The motion is *compact*. The hip stays quiet. The knee leads.

Tricks with leggy dexes tend to feel *contained* — the upper body stays composed, the supporting leg holds its position, and the dex reads as a tight knee-driven trace.

**The canonical leggy-dex tricks are Legover, Pickup, Whirl, Swirl, and Illusion.** Legover is a leggy out. Pickup is a leggy in. Whirl is a leggy in (landing on clipper instead of toe). Swirl is a leggy out across the body. Illusion is a leggy out (landing on the opposite toe).

### Why hippy vs leggy matters

This is the framework that makes the rest of the trick vocabulary navigable.

When you watch a Mirage and a Pickup back-to-back, you can see the difference: the Mirage swings from the hip (chest opens, thigh rotates) while the Pickup pivots from the knee (chest stays composed, calf traces a tight circle). Both are *in*-direction dexes — the leg travels in the same direction relative to the body. But the *body region driving the motion* differs.

When you watch a Mirage and an Illusion side-by-side, the difference is even clearer. Both are mirror moves to an opposite-side toe — same destination, same general shape. But Mirage is hippy (the thigh swings) and Illusion is leggy (the calf circles). The trick is named differently because the body is doing something different, even though the surface trajectory of the bag is similar.

This distinction generalizes:

- **Mirage (hippy in) vs Pickup (leggy in)** — same direction, different body region.
- **Butterfly (hippy out) vs Legover (leggy out)** — same direction, different body region.
- **Mirage vs Illusion** — same destination, same direction, but hippy vs leggy.

If you take nothing else from this primer, take this: **the hippy / leggy distinction is the body-mechanics axis underneath the vocabulary.** It explains why Mirage and Illusion are different tricks. It explains why Butterfly and Whirl don't feel the same to do. It will keep coming back in every section that follows.

## §1.6 In and out — the direction the leg travels

A dex moves in one of two **directions** relative to the centerline of your body:

- **in** (in-out, or "in" for short): the leg starts away from the body's centerline and circles toward and through. From "in" the body to "out" of the body.
- **out** (out-in, or "out" for short): the leg starts away and circles toward the body. From "out" of the body to "in."

The two directions feel different in the hip and the knee. An in-direction hippy dex (Mirage) sweeps the thigh from the supporting hip's far side, across the body's midline, and back. An out-direction hippy dex (Butterfly) sweeps the thigh the other way — the leg arcs away from the support, around the bag, and back.

You'll see this pairing throughout the vocabulary:

- **Mirage (hippy in) and Butterfly (hippy out)** — same body region, opposite directions.
- **Pickup (leggy in) and Legover (leggy out)** — same body region, opposite directions.
- **Whirl (leggy in) and Swirl (leggy out)** — same body region, opposite directions, both to clipper.

Most of the named base tricks come in **direction-paired families**: a hippy in and a hippy out, a leggy in and a leggy out. The pairing isn't accidental. The community named them as pairs because the body does them as pairs.

## §1.7 The timing clock — uptime, midtime, downtime

A single trick has a clock with three windows. Once the bag leaves your foot, it's *up*. It rises, it peaks, it falls. The trick's components fit into the windows:

- **Uptime** — the period while the bag is moving up. Components that happen during uptime are called *sets* (because they set up what follows). A *stepping* uptime, for example, is a foot relocation that happens while the bag is rising.
- **Midtime** — the brief peak, when the bag is momentarily neither rising nor falling. Often empty; sometimes used for spins, ducks, or no-plant body movements.
- **Downtime** — the period while the bag is falling. The dex you've been reading about in §1.5 is usually a downtime component. The catch lives at the end of downtime.

Every trick fits some pattern of *what fills each window*. A Mirage is `[set] → downtime hippy in dex → catch on opposite toe`. A Ripwalk is `[set + stepping uptime] → downtime hippy out dex → catch on same-side clipper`. The clock is the framework; the trick's components are what fill it.

When you watch a player call out a trick by name, what they're describing is *which window holds which component*. That's why the names get long when there are many components stacked — each component fills a window, and the name has to mention each window's filler.

## §1.8 Reading a trick

Put the pieces together. Here's the operational notation for Mirage:

```
[set] > in dex > op toe
```

Read it left to right:

- `[set]` — start with the bag set into the air, via the set foot.
- `in dex` — a hippy in-direction dex happens during downtime. (The notation doesn't carry the word *hippy* — it's implicit in Mirage's body mechanics.)
- `op toe` — the catch is an opposite-side toe stall.

Three tokens. Three things to know. Compare that to the operational reading of Ripwalk:

```
[clip] > op in dex > butterfly out dex > ss clipper
```

(Ripwalk is the structural reading "Stepping Butterfly" expanded operationally.) Four tokens because Ripwalk has both an uptime dex (the stepping component) and a downtime dex (the butterfly out). The reading still goes left to right: start, uptime, downtime, catch.

Once you can read this notation as a structural sentence — entry surface, what happens during uptime, what happens during downtime, catch surface — you've crossed the threshold the primer is trying to get you to.

## §1.9 Modifiers transform what's already there

There's a small set of words that don't name tricks but *transform* them. Add **stepping** to a Butterfly and you get a Ripwalk: the original Butterfly is still there, but the uptime now carries a stepping component. Add **spinning** to a Torque (which is itself a structural reading of "miraging osis") and you get a Mobius: the original Torque is still there, but the body rotates a full turn during the dex.

This is the **operator** way of thinking about modifiers. A modifier isn't a word in a glossary; it's a transformation that takes a base trick and produces a new trick by changing one window in the clock. Stepping fills the uptime. Spinning carries through the dex. Paradox is a hip pivot between two dexes. Ducking is a head dip between two dexes. Each one is a *function* that takes a base and produces a compound.

This primer doesn't enumerate the modifiers; §6 of the glossary does that. But the framing matters here: when you see a name like *Paradox Symposium Whirl*, you should hear three operators applied to a Whirl base. Paradox transforms one of the windows. Symposium transforms another. Whirl is the structural anchor. The name reads as the composition.

## §1.10 What you can do now

If you've followed the primer this far, you can:

- Recognize that every trick has the same five-beat skeleton.
- Identify hippy versus leggy in a dex by watching which body region drives it.
- Pair direction (in / out) with body style (hippy / leggy) to classify any dex you see.
- Read a side flag (ss / op) as a relationship between two consecutive components, not an absolute side of the body.
- Map a trick's components onto the timing clock (uptime / midtime / downtime).
- Read operational notation as a left-to-right structural sentence.
- Hear a name like *Spinning Paradox Whirl* as three operators applied to a base, not as three opaque words.

That's enough to start reading the dictionary as a structured catalog instead of a list of names. The rest of the glossary — §2 through §9 — fills out the vocabulary. §2 covers contact surfaces in full. §3 covers dexterities in detail (including how to recognize hippy and leggy in motion). §4 formalizes the timing clock. §5 gives you each base trick's notation, body mechanics, and structural relatives in one place. §6 enumerates the modifier-operators. §7 covers the notation language formally. §8 covers how tricks combine into strings. §9 introduces the topology layer that lets you see tricks across families.

Pick a starting point. You can read the rest in order, or skip to whichever section matches what you're trying to do next.

## §1.11 A note on this primer's stance

Two things you won't find here:

**No claim that tricks are "really" their compositions.** A Mirage *is* a Mirage; it's also readable as a hippy in dex to an opposite toe. Both are true. The name carries cultural weight; the composition carries structural information. The primer teaches the composition because composition is what's *learnable*. The name remains the name.

**No claim that the symbolic-grammar layer replaces the dictionary.** The dictionary is the canonical source for which tricks exist, what their ADD values are, and how the community classifies them into families. The primer's job is to make that dictionary readable — not to outvote it. When this primer says "Mirage is hippy," that's a body-mechanics observation; the dictionary's family classification still owns the canonical structure.

The glossary teaches the language. The dictionary catalogs the tricks. Both are necessary; neither replaces the other.
