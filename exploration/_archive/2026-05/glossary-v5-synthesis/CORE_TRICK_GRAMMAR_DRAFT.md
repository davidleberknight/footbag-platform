# CORE_TRICK_GRAMMAR_DRAFT

**Project:** GLOSSARY-V5-SYNTHESIS — Task C
**Scope:** Draft prose for §5 of the V5 glossary architecture — the core trick structures. Ten canonical base tricks, each with operational notation, dex class (hippy / leggy / other), body-mechanics prose, and structural relatives. Notation supports the explanation; the body-mechanics prose carries the meaning.
**Constraints:** Notation conventions per the curator-supplied canonical form. No invented tokens (e.g., "butterfly wing" is not used as a notation token because it has not been canonically defined as one).

---

## How to read this section

Each entry below is a **base trick** — a named structure that other named tricks descend from, either directly (as compounds layered on top) or by being its mirror or direction-paired sibling. The entries are in the order the primer introduces base concepts: from the simplest stalls upward, through the toe tricks, into the clipper tricks.

Each entry has the same shape:

- **Notation.** A compact operational reading of the trick.
- **Dex class.** Hippy, leggy, or another classification (some entries are not dex-driven).
- **Body mechanics.** A paragraph describing what the body actually does. This is what makes a learner internalize the trick.
- **Structural relatives.** Trick names that share enough structure to be in the same neighbourhood.

Read these entries with the primer's hippy / leggy framework already in your head. Every body-mechanics paragraph will lean on it.

## §5.1 Toe Stall

```
toe
```

**Dex class:** none — a toe stall is a contact, not a dex.

**Body mechanics.** The bag rests briefly on the top of the shoe, on the toe surface. The kicking foot is up, knee bent, ankle stable; the support leg holds the body's weight. The toe surface is angled slightly upward to receive the bag and hold it momentarily. The motion isn't really a motion — it's a *pause* the bag makes on the surface before the next thing happens.

The toe stall is the simplest building block in the language. Every toe trick ends in one; every dex that runs toward a toe surface lands on this stall. It's where the language begins.

**Structural relatives:** Clipper Stall (the same idea on the inside surface); kick (the same surface, but with motion instead of pause).

---

## §5.2 Clipper Stall

```
ss clipper
```

**Dex class:** none — a stall.

**Body mechanics.** The clipper is the inside surface of the shoe, but with the legs in a **crossbody position** — one leg crossed in front of the other. To hold a clipper, you crank or bend the ankle so the inside surface flexes upward, becoming a small flat platform the bag can rest on. The supporting leg holds steady; the kicking leg is the crossed one.

The crossbody position is the whole reason this surface gets its own name. An inside stall with the legs uncrossed is just an inside stall. The clipper *requires* the crossbody.

**Structural relatives:** Inside Stall (same surface, different stance); Toe Stall (the other foundational stall surface, parallel role in the language).

---

## §5.3 ATW — Around the World

```
toe > ss in dex > ss toe
```

**Dex class:** hippy or leggy depending on the player — but most often hippy in.

**Body mechanics.** Start with the bag on one toe. Set the bag up. The kicking leg — typically the same leg that was holding the bag — circles the bag once in the *in* direction. The leg returns to the same toe to catch.

The "around the world" name describes what the leg does: it makes a full circle around the bag, ending up where it started. The same-side, same-foot character is what distinguishes ATW from most other dex tricks. The body stays mostly stationary; the leg does the circling.

ATW is one of the older named structures in the freestyle vocabulary. It's structurally simple — one dex, one stall, one side — but it taught the community what a *dex* was. Almost every later trick in the vocabulary is a variation, modification, or composition of the dex action ATW codified.

**Structural relatives:** Orbit (the same trick with an out-direction dex); DATW (Double ATW, two consecutive in dexes on the same side); around-the-world variants on other surfaces.

---

## §5.4 Orbit (the reverse of ATW)

```
toe > ss out dex > ss toe
```

**Dex class:** hippy or leggy depending on the player — but most often hippy out.

**Body mechanics.** Same setup as ATW: bag on toe, set, dex, return to the same toe. The difference is the *direction* of the circle. An ATW goes in (across the body and back); an Orbit goes out (away from the body and back).

The two are direction-paired siblings: same destination, same body region, opposite leg path. A player who can do ATW comfortably usually finds Orbit follows quickly, because the body knows the shape — it just has to learn the opposite arc.

**Structural relatives:** ATW (the in-direction sibling); any out-direction dex on a same-side toe.

## §5.5 Legover

```
[set] > out dex > ss toe
```

**Dex class:** leggy.

**Body mechanics.** The set is the prepatory uptime; the body sends the bag into the air. During downtime, the kicking leg traces an out-direction circle around the bag — but the motion is driven by the **knee**, not the hip. The thigh stays relatively stable; the calf and lower leg swing out around the bag's path and return.

This is the canonical leggy-out dex. The body region driving the motion (knee, not hip) and the direction (out) together place Legover in its specific corner of the dex space. The catch is on the same-side toe — the leg returns to where the set started.

Legover is one of the LIMP tricks (Legover, Illusion, Mirage, Pickup) — the four canonical toe-tricks that newcomers learn together because they share the basic toe-set, dex, toe-catch shape.

**Structural relatives:** Pickup (the leggy-in sibling, mirror direction); ATW (the same-direction sibling but same-foot full-circle); any leggy-out dex on another base.

---

## §5.6 Pickup (the reverse of Legover)

```
[set] > in dex > ss toe
```

**Dex class:** leggy.

**Body mechanics.** Same setup, opposite direction. The set sends the bag up; during downtime the kicking leg traces an *in*-direction circle, driven again by the **knee**. The calf does the work; the thigh stays composed. The catch is on the same-side toe.

Pickup is paired with Legover in the LIMP family. Both are leggy; they differ only in direction. A player who's comfortable with one is usually fast at picking up the other — same body region, same surface destination, opposite leg path.

The "pickup" name captures something about the body feel: the leg seems to *pick up* the bag's path on the inward side and trace it home. Some players describe Pickup as more "tucked" than Legover (the leg comes in close to the body), while Legover is more "extended" (the leg swings outward).

**Structural relatives:** Legover (the direction-paired sibling); Mirage (the *hippy*-in sibling — same direction, different body region).

## §5.7 Mirage

```
[set] > in dex > op toe
```

**Dex class:** hippy.

**Body mechanics.** The set sends the bag up; during downtime the kicking leg traces an *in*-direction circle around the bag — but here the motion comes from the **hip**. The thigh sweeps from the body's far side, across the centerline, around the bag, and finishes on the opposite side. The hip opens; the chest tends to rotate to follow.

The motion is broad, openhanded. Where Pickup is tucked (the calf circles tight), Mirage is open (the thigh sweeps wide). Where Pickup lands ss (same-side toe), Mirage lands op (opposite-side toe) — the wider hippy motion naturally carries the leg across the body, so the toe it lands on is the opposite one.

This is one of the most-taught tricks in freestyle. Almost every player learns Mirage early because it sits at the structural center of the language: an op-toe trick with a hippy in-dex, paired with Pickup (the leggy-in sibling) and Illusion (the op-toe mirror with a leggy-out dex). The structural neighborhood around Mirage is wide.

The implicit default for "Toe Mirage" is the op version. When someone says "Mirage" without specifying a side, this is what they mean.

**Structural relatives:** Illusion (the leggy-out sibling, mirror of Mirage); Pickup (the leggy-in sibling, same direction, different body region); Paradox Mirage (Mirage with a paradox hip pivot inserted); Smear (Mirage with a different uptime treatment).

---

## §5.8 Illusion (the reverse of Mirage)

```
[set] > out dex > op toe
```

**Dex class:** leggy.

**Body mechanics.** The set sends the bag up; during downtime the kicking leg traces an *out*-direction circle, driven by the **knee** — the calf circles outward around the bag's path and returns. The catch is on the opposite-side toe.

Here's where the language reveals its precision: Illusion and Mirage have the same destination (op toe), the same general visual shape (a leg circling around the bag on its way to the opposite foot), but the **body region driving the motion is different**. Mirage swings the thigh from the hip; Illusion pivots the calf from the knee. Watching them back-to-back makes the difference visible — Mirage opens the chest; Illusion keeps the chest composed.

This is why Illusion has its own name despite being so structurally close to Mirage. The community named it as a distinct trick because the body is doing something distinct.

**Structural relatives:** Mirage (the hippy-in sibling, mirror of Illusion); Legover (the leggy-out sibling on a same-side toe); any leggy-out dex.

---

## §5.9 Butterfly

```
[set] > out dex > ss clipper
```

**Dex class:** hippy.

**Body mechanics.** The set sends the bag up; during downtime the kicking leg traces an *out*-direction circle, driven by the **hip**. The thigh swings outward from the supporting hip's near side, around the bag's path, and back. The chest tends to open to follow the motion. The catch is on a same-side clipper — the inside surface of the shoe, in a crossbody position.

Butterfly is the canonical hippy-out dex, and the canonical entry to the clipper-tricks half of the vocabulary. Where Mirage taught you what a hippy in dex feels like, Butterfly teaches you what a hippy out dex feels like. The body region is the same (hip-driven thigh swing); the direction is opposite.

Butterfly's compounds are some of the densest in freestyle. Stepping Butterfly is a Ripwalk. Pixie Butterfly is part of the Phoenix decomposition. Atomic Butterfly, Ducking Butterfly — the butterfly base is a heavy compositional anchor. When you read a name with "butterfly" in it, you should hear: hippy out dex to a same-side clipper.

The implicit default for "Clipper Butterfly" is ss. When someone says "Butterfly" without specifying a side, this is what they mean.

**Structural relatives:** Whirl (the leggy sibling on the same clipper destination); Mirage (the hippy-in sibling, opposite direction, opposite catch surface); Ripwalk (Stepping Butterfly); Phoenix (Pixie Ducking Butterfly); Dimwalk (Pixie Butterfly).

## §5.10 Osis

```
[set] > spin > ss clipper
```

**Dex class:** not a single dex — Osis is built around a **body spin** rather than a leg-driven dex.

**Body mechanics.** The set sends the bag up; during the trick, the *body* rotates around its vertical axis. The catch is on a same-side clipper. What makes Osis distinctive is that the trick is named for the body's rotational motion, not for a leg's circling motion. The dex window is occupied by a *body action* — a spin — rather than a knee or hip swing.

This is structurally interesting because most freestyle tricks are named for what the *leg* does. Osis is named for what the *whole body* does. Tricks that descend from Osis tend to inherit the rotational character: Torque is a "miraging osis" — Osis with a mirage-style dex layered on. Mobius is a "spinning torque" — Torque with another full rotation added. The rotational character is the through-line.

Some players don't classify Osis as either hippy or leggy because the leg's motion isn't the trick's identity — the body's rotation is. Other players read Osis as fundamentally hippy (the support hip pivots through the rotation). The classification is debated; the rotational identity is not.

**Structural relatives:** Torque (Osis with miraging layered on); Mobius (Spinning Torque); Blender (a whirling-osis canonical reading); Spender (a compound on blender base); Paradox Osis and Paradox Blender (with paradox hip pivot inserted).

## §5.11 Whirl

```
[set] > in dex > ss clipper
```

**Dex class:** leggy.

**Body mechanics.** The set sends the bag up; during downtime the kicking leg traces an *in*-direction circle around the bag, driven by the **knee**. The calf does the work; the thigh stays relatively quiet. The catch is on a same-side clipper.

Whirl is the canonical leggy-in dex on a clipper destination. Mirage is the hippy parallel on a toe destination; Whirl is the leggy parallel on a clipper destination. Both are in-direction dexes; both are commonly taught early; but their body regions and surface destinations differ.

This is where the symmetry of the language becomes visible. The four canonical entries into the "dex with side default" space:

- **Mirage** (hippy in → op toe)
- **Pickup** (leggy in → ss toe)
- **Butterfly** (hippy out → ss clipper)
- **Whirl** (leggy in → ss clipper)

Each one occupies a different corner of the *body region × direction × surface* cube. Together they form the structural backbone the rest of the trick vocabulary descends from.

Whirl is also the rotational base of one of the largest trick families in freestyle: Spinning Whirl, Paradox Whirl, Ducking Whirl, Stepping Whirl, Mullet, Montage, and a dozen others. The whirl base appears in more dense compounds than any other single base trick.

**Structural relatives:** Swirl (the leggy-out sibling); Butterfly (the hippy sibling on the same clipper destination); Mirage (the hippy parallel on a toe destination); Spinning Whirl (Whirl with full-body rotation through the dex); Paradox Whirl; Ducking Whirl; Montage.

## §5.12 Swirl (the reverse of Whirl)

```
{set} > (xbd) out dex > ss clipper
```

**Dex class:** leggy.

**Body mechanics.** The set sends the bag up; during downtime the kicking leg traces an *out*-direction circle around the bag — driven by the **knee** — but with the body in a **crossbody position** entering the dex. The `(xbd)` flag in the notation marks the pre-dex stance: one leg is already crossed in front of the other before the dex happens. The catch is on a same-side clipper.

The crossbody-stance entry is what gives Swirl its distinct character. Whirl's leggy-in dex doesn't require any unusual pre-positioning; Swirl's leggy-out dex requires the body to already be in a crossbody position so that the *out* dex can circle the bag in front of the body without the legs interfering.

This is also why Swirl naturally finishes on a same-side clipper — the body is already set up for the clipper catch by the crossbody stance. The trick reads as a tight, controlled motion with the legs maintaining their crossed relationship throughout.

**Structural relatives:** Whirl (the in-direction sibling); Butterfly (the hippy parallel — same direction, opposite body region); any out-direction dex with a crossbody requirement; Swirling-dex compounds like Front Swirl and Back Swirl variants.

## §5.13 The structural map

These ten core entries form the backbone of the freestyle vocabulary. Mapped along the two key axes:

| Direction | Body region | Surface destination | Trick |
|---|---|---|---|
| in | hippy | op toe | **Mirage** |
| in | leggy | ss toe | **Pickup** |
| in | leggy | ss clipper | **Whirl** |
| out | hippy | ss clipper | **Butterfly** |
| out | leggy | ss toe | **Legover** |
| out | leggy | op toe | **Illusion** |
| out | leggy | ss clipper (xbd) | **Swirl** |
| in (full circle) | varies | ss toe (same foot) | **ATW** |
| out (full circle) | varies | ss toe (same foot) | **Orbit** |
| body spin | n/a | ss clipper | **Osis** |

The map reveals the structural symmetry. The dex space is mostly *paired*: every in-direction trick has an out-direction sibling; every hippy trick has a leggy parallel; every ss trick has an op variant somewhere in the catalog. The named base tricks fill the corners of the cube the community found most useful.

This is the foundation. Every compound trick the dictionary catalogs — from Ripwalk to Mobius to Phoenix to Montage — descends from one or more of these ten bases by way of the modifier-operators in §6.

## §5.14 What this section does NOT cover

These ten entries are the **core structures**. The glossary does not (and should not) duplicate the dictionary's full catalog here. The dictionary is the canonical home for compound tricks like Ripwalk, Phoenix, Mobius, Montage, Paradox Whirl, and so on. This section's job is to give you the **bases** the dictionary's compounds descend from.

When you read a compound name in the dictionary — for example, "Spinning Ducking Paradox Whirl" — you should be able to point to *Whirl* in this section as the structural anchor, recognize each prefix as a modifier-operator from §6, and read the name as a composition. The compound itself lives in the dictionary; the language for reading it lives here.
