## 1. dada-curve — 4 ADD, dada-curve family (standalone)

**Rationale.** Standalone-with-records (Anssi Sundberg 2013). Dual-anchor framing: **sidewalk (SCALE-8 pilot)** carries the semantic kinship — both are 4-ADD self-atoms in the butterfly-walking family with no canonical modifier+base decomposition; **ripwalk (SCALE-5 pilot)** carries the operational-mechanics contrast — the same `clip-dex-dex-op clip` set-arc shape, differentiated by whether the support leg relocates during execution. The two cross-references work on different axes: sidewalk grounds dada-curve as a recognized walking-family row; ripwalk teaches the foot-plant discipline by direct contrast.

**short_description**
A self-atom in the butterfly-walking family — same set-arc shape as ripwalk's stepping wing-pattern, but executed with the supporting foot stationary through the entire trick.

**execution_summary**
The opening kick lands same-side outside in butterfly's wing pattern. Two dex flicks carry the bag's path through the body line as the wing motion completes; the recovery reaches opposite-side and lands in an opposite clipper cross-body delay. End-to-end, the supporting foot stays planted where it began.

Sidewalk and ripwalk run variations on the same set-arc shape but with the support relocating between kicks — sidewalk holding same-side direction across the stepping motion, ripwalk taking the natural-direction wing. Dada-curve preserves the kick sequence while removing the step entirely; the body absorbs the full wing-arc travel without the help of a relocated base, and the trick reads as its own family rather than as a stepping variant.

**learning_notes**
Holding the support stationary through the wing motion fights the natural impulse to step. The body wants to redistribute weight as the kicking foot completes the arc; resisting that impulse keeps the trick honest. If the support shifts even slightly during the wing transition, the trick crosses into ripwalk territory and the dada-curve identity is lost.

Watch the planted foot, not the kicking foot. A clean dada-curve shows zero foot translation during the bag's travel — only weight transfer through the leg and hip. Any pivot or shuffle of the support pulls the compound out of its own family and into one of the stepping-butterfly variants.

**prerequisite_notes**
Butterfly (3 ADD, SCALE-6 pilot) underwrites the wing-motion vocabulary. Sidewalk (4 ADD, SCALE-8 pilot) is the closest semantic sibling — both rows are 4-ADD self-atoms in the butterfly-walking family, neither carrying a clean modifier+base canonical decomposition. Ripwalk (4 ADD, SCALE-5 pilot) is the operational-mechanics sibling — same opening kick, same recovery, same set-arc shape; the foot-plant decision is what separates the two. The three rows together form a small cluster: butterfly as the base, ripwalk and sidewalk as stepping variants at different body-direction commitments, dada-curve as the no-step variant.

## 2. eclipse — 3 ADD, eclipse family (named compound)

**Rationale.** Standalone-with-records (Yosshi Yamamoto 2020, Ken Somolinos 2013 — both passback). The "catwalk" alias carries historical naming weight worth surfacing without leaning on it. Lead with mechanics; the alias enters mid-paragraph.

**short_description**
A three-arc dex pattern that carries the bag through a single rotation — the kicking foot traces an opening clip, a mid-air dex, and a return clip in one composed motion, the body completing a quarter-turn across the sequence.

**execution_summary**
The opening kick lands as a clip on the bag's outside line. The same foot continues into a dex flick at the apex of the bag's path — a quick redirect that holds the rotation through the body's quarter-turn. The motion completes with a return clip on the opposite side, the body squared to the new rotation.

The three arcs read as one composed trick rather than as three separate kicks. The dex flick at the apex is the load-bearing element; without it, the two clips fall into a generic dex compound and the named 3-ADD identity dissolves.

**learning_notes**
The historical name catwalk traces the rotational footwork — the supporting leg's path during the body's quarter-turn resembles a deliberate catwalk step rather than a freestyle pivot. The catwalk reading captures the body's intent: the rotation is composed and unhurried, the foot path matching the bag's arc rather than chasing it.

The most frequent error is rushing the mid-air dex. The dex needs time to register as a distinct kick — if it blends into the opening clip's recovery, the trick reads as a clip-into-clip with no rotational meaning, and the asserted 3 ADD becomes a courtesy. Slow the apex deliberately; the rotation will catch up.

**prerequisite_notes**
Clipper (foundation) anchors the opening and closing arcs. The mid-air dex flick borrows from any 1-ADD dex primitive (basic dex, mirage, illusion). Eclipse sits at the entry tier of named 3-ADD compounds — comparable in difficulty to other 3-ADD self-atom rows like terrage and reverse-drifter (this batch), but distinguished by the explicit rotational accounting carried by the apex dex.

## 3. jani-walker — 5 ADD, jani-walker family (barraging-butterfly canonical)

**Rationale.** Standalone-with-records (Jim Penske 2015, David Clavens 2006 — both passback, ~9 years apart). Records' sort_name "Barraging Butterfly (op)" reveals jani-walker as the named-trick form of barraging-butterfly. Math closes via row-specific rotational inference on butterfly under the canonicalization-policy "stated ADD without stated structure" disposition — same class as nemesis (SCALE-9). Confront the math directly; pair the framing with nemesis for cohort coherence.

**short_description**
Barraging-butterfly under its named form — the butterfly wing-motion carrying a barraging rotational intensity, the compound resolving to 5 ADD via row-specific accounting.

**execution_summary**
The opening kick traces the standard butterfly wing arc with a barraging body commitment layered through the motion. The kicking foot's path follows the outside-wing trajectory while the body carries a rotational intensity that goes beyond butterfly's baseline; the second kick arrives with the rotation already in progress.

The barraging set is the load-bearing element. Without it, the trick falls into a clean 3-ADD butterfly; with it, the body's rotational accounting raises the compound to 5 ADD. The recovery lands on the opposite clipper with the rotation complete, the body squared to its new orientation.

**learning_notes**
The barraging intensity is committed before the first kick, not added during execution. Treating barraging as something layered onto an in-progress butterfly produces a smeared compound that reads neither as clean butterfly nor as clean barraging-butterfly. The set begins with the rotational commitment already in the body.

Jani-walker sits in a small cohort of named compounds whose decomposition is recorded in the canonical name and the records' sort label rather than in a separate modifier table. Nemesis (SCALE-9 pilot) carries the same shape: a stated ADD reading that closes via row-specific modifier accounting. The two rows are paired teaching cases for how the dictionary handles compounds whose modifier classification is implicit rather than table-driven.

**prerequisite_notes**
Butterfly (3 ADD, SCALE-6 pilot) provides the wing-motion base. The barraging intensity is best approached after fury (5 ADD, SCALE-7 pilot) and nemesis (6 ADD, SCALE-9 pilot) — both compounds where a rotational modifier composes onto a base via row-specific math. Once those rows hold, jani-walker becomes the barraging analog: the same modifier-accounting discipline applied to butterfly's wing-motion vocabulary.

## 4. reverse-drifter — 3 ADD, reverse-drifter family (direction-reverse)

**Rationale.** Direction-reversed drifter; separate canonical row per the direction-is-structural rule. No records on file — the editorial hook is the canonical-row distinction, not athlete provenance. Foundation framing without invoking the retired "is the foundation" phrasing; future bridge pilots (paradox-reverse-drifter, pixie-reverse-drifter) would land here.

**short_description**
The miraging-clipper line executed in the opposite directional travel — same kick shape as drifter, the body's path reversed across the canonical dex structure.

**execution_summary**
The opening kick traces the miraging-clipper arc (drifter's canonical structure per the dictionary) but with the body traveling in the opposite directional sense — the bag's path reading from outside-to-inside relative to the body, where standard drifter reads inside-to-outside. The same kicking foot, the same dex shape, the body's lateral travel reversed.

The recovery lands with the body oriented opposite to a drifter's recovery position. The trick's identity carries through the directional commitment: the kick mechanics alone do not differentiate reverse-drifter from drifter; only the body's travel path does.

**learning_notes**
Direction is a canonical-row determiner in the dictionary, not a stylistic preference. A drifter executed with the body drifting the wrong way is reverse-drifter, not a sloppy drifter — and vice versa. The two tricks share their kick shape but the body's path through space defines which row a given execution actually lands on.

The error most worth watching for is uncommitted direction. A body that drifts only slightly in the named direction will read as a partial-drifter without clean canonical identity; the dictionary expects the directional commitment to be unambiguous. Commit to the direction before the kick, not during.

**prerequisite_notes**
Drifter (3 ADD, SCALE-5 pilot) is the direct twin — canonical miraging-clipper, opposite directional travel. Clipper (foundation) and mirage (2 ADD, SCALE-6 pilot) underwrite the structural vocabulary. Once a learner can execute drifter cleanly with directional intent, reverse-drifter becomes the discipline of executing the same kick shape with the opposite body commitment; the two rows together establish the direction-is-structural rule in practice rather than in the abstract.

## 5. ripstein — 4 ADD, ripstein family (double-swirl same-side)

**Rationale.** Standalone-with-records (Evan Gatesman 2013 + 2015 — same athlete, 2-year gap, consistency hook). Records' sort_name "Swirl (ss) (double)" reveals ripstein as the named-trick form of same-side double swirl. ss qualifier is execution-direction only (not ADD-additive per canonical math); double-swirl is one of the multiplicity-rule community-stabilized exceptions. Surface as canonical math without referencing the policy-version source.

**short_description**
Two consecutive swirls run same-side — the same rotational pattern executed twice in succession off a single set, the body completing both rotations before recovery.

**execution_summary**
The set arrives with enough height to carry two full rotational passes. The first swirl runs the standard rotational pattern; before the bag drops, the kicking foot initiates the second swirl on the same side as the first. The body holds the rotational axis through both passes — the second swirl mirrors the first's mechanics without resetting the body's orientation.

The recovery lands on the opposite-side delay with both rotations complete. The same-side execution distinguishes ripstein from a cross-body double — the rotational axis stays on one side of the body throughout, the two swirls composing into a single sustained rotational sequence.

**learning_notes**
The set height is the load-bearing element. A standard swirl-height set leaves no time for the second pass; ripstein requires a set that gives the body two rotational windows. Set too low, and the second swirl arrives after the bag drops; set too high, and the rotational timing decouples from the bag's path.

Same-side execution is the canonical identity. A double swirl run cross-body is a different trick — the same-side discipline is what makes the row ripstein rather than a generic double-rotation compound. The body's commitment to one side throughout the sequence is more important than the rotational mechanics themselves.

**prerequisite_notes**
Swirl (3 ADD, SCALE-9 pilot) is the single-pass base. The multiplicity discipline (running the same trick twice off one set without resetting body position) is the canonical analog of double-leg-over (3 ADD, SCALE-9 pilot) and double-around-the-world — dictionary rows where a community-stabilized doubling convention earns its own named-row treatment rather than collapsing into a "double X" descriptor.

## 6. terrage — 4 ADD, terrage family (parallel-to-barrage)

**Rationale.** Standalone-with-records (Anssi Sundberg 2008 — passback). Own mechanical family parallel to barrage. Trick-vs-modifier distinction is load-bearing: terrage = standalone trick at 4 ADD; terraging = modifier action at +3 ADD per the modifier table. Prose must distinguish to prevent reader confusion on pages like Terraging Leg Over (terraging+legover=3+3=6).

**short_description**
Mechanically parallel to barrage but standing on its own as a 4-ADD trick — the same rotational-intensity family with terrage carrying its own canonical execution distinct from the terraging modifier's compound-building role.

**execution_summary**
The set initiates with the terrage rotational commitment — a body intensity that runs parallel to barrage's set-character but with terrage's own specific kick sequence. The opening kick carries the rotational arc; the body completes the rotation through a held line; the recovery lands on the opposite-side delay with the rotational accounting closed.

The trick is a single composed execution rather than a base + modifier compound. Where Terraging Leg Over (terraging applied to legover) reads as 3 + 3 = 6 ADD via the standard modifier table, terrage as a standalone reads as 4 ADD via its own family accounting — the trick form and the modifier form carry different ADD profiles and different mechanical commitments.

**learning_notes**
The naming proximity to the terraging modifier is the most common source of confusion. A learner who has seen Terraging Leg Over may expect terrage to behave like a modifier — applying to some base. Terrage does not work this way: it is a complete trick whose 4 ADD belongs to the row, not to a composition.

The mechanical parallel to barrage runs deeper than naming. Both tricks share a rotational-intensity family that distinguishes them from spinning-family rows on one side and from gyro-family rows on the other; the terrage / barrage parallel is a vocabulary axis in its own right, and learners who treat the two as interchangeable miss the family-specific body discipline each requires.

**prerequisite_notes**
Barrage (foundation) is the mechanical parallel — the canonical reference point for terrage's family of body discipline. The terraging modifier (covered on the modifier reference surface, not on this page) carries the +3 ADD action used in compounds like Terraging Leg Over; reading terrage as a trick and terraging as a modifier separately is the cleanest entry to both. No standard modifier-base bridge for terrage exists yet at pilot tier — the row stands on its own family.
