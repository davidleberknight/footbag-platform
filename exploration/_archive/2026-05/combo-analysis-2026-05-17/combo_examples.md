# Combo Examples — Worked Walkthroughs

Slice: Combo Analysis extraction (2026-05-17). Pedagogical examples translating v6 network findings into readable freestyle commentary.

## Why worked examples

The v6 report carries findings as statistics: "blurry whirl → whirl is the most common two-trick transition (17×, 15 players, 14 events)." That sentence is true but doesn't *explain* anything to a reader who hasn't internalized why. Worked examples flip the perspective: here is a sequence, here is what the player is doing, here is why this architecture works.

Each example below is grounded in a real corpus pattern and surfaces 2–4 concepts from `RUN_ARCHITECTURE_GLOSSARY_PLAN.md`.

## Example 1 — Blurry Whirl → Whirl (the canonical pair)

```
blurry whirl  →  whirl
   5 ADD          3 ADD
   = 8 ADD total
```

### What's happening

The player opens with the hardest single rotational element in regular use (blurry whirl, 5 ADD), then resolves to whirl's stable clipper-stall landing.

### Why this architecture works

- **Difficulty announcement.** Opening with the high-ADD element signals competitive intent immediately. The judge sees the commitment in the first beat.
- **Risk-management.** Resolving to whirl gives the body a stable termination on a familiar surface. The hardest moment of the combo is also the moment when fatigue is lowest.
- **Mechanical compatibility.** Both tricks live in the whirl family; the body position at the end of blurry whirl flows directly into whirl's entry.

### Concepts surfaced
- **Setup vs resolution** — but inverted: the "setup" here is the hardest element, and resolution carries lower ADD but higher stability
- **Launch node** — blurry whirl is the corpus's most documented launch node
- **Attractor** — whirl is the corpus's strongest attractor
- **Clipper stabilization** — both tricks land on clipper

### Frequency in corpus
17 documented appearances across 15 players and 14 events. The single most common two-trick transition.

## Example 2 — Smear → Dimwalk → Ripwalk (the walking ladder)

```
smear  →  dimwalk  →  ripwalk  →  (whirl or blur)
 ?         4 ADD       4 ADD
```

### What's happening

A walking-trick chain. Smear opens with a low-ADD movement, dimwalk extends through a 4-ADD throughput, ripwalk continues the walking pattern, and the chain typically resolves to a rotational element afterward.

### Why this architecture works

- **Throughput pacing.** Dimwalk has high outbound transition count and low inbound count — it gives more than it takes. Players reach for it specifically because it leads somewhere.
- **Family consistency.** The walking family (smear / dimwalk / ripwalk / parkwalk) shares mechanical conventions; chaining within a family reduces cognitive load mid-sequence.
- **Resolution flexibility.** The chain doesn't commit to a specific terminus; the player chooses whirl, blur, or another rotational element based on body state at exit.

### Concepts surfaced
- **Throughput trick** — dimwalk
- **Walking transitions** — the family-level connector pattern
- **Setup chain** — three setup tricks before resolution
- **Stabilization** — the walking family acts as composure-management between rotational work

### Frequency in corpus
smear → dimwalk: 7× across 7 players. The walking ladder is well-attested.

## Example 3 — Butterfly → Blurry Whirl (cross-family launch)

```
butterfly   →   blurry whirl   →   (resolution)
  3 ADD            5 ADD
```

### What's happening

Butterfly opens at moderate ADD, then the player escalates immediately to blurry whirl. The chain extends with whatever resolution the body permits.

### Why this architecture works

- **Setup that creates space.** Butterfly's mechanics free the body for the high-rotation entry that blurry whirl demands. Without an entry move, blurry whirl is much harder to initiate cleanly.
- **Cross-family transition.** Butterfly and blurry whirl are in different families. The chain demonstrates compositional vocabulary mastery — players prove they can move between movement systems within a single sequence.
- **Difficulty escalation.** Unlike Example 1's flat 5→3 architecture, this opens at 3 and escalates to 5 — a "rising" sequence.

### Concepts surfaced
- **Setup trick** — butterfly is the canonical setup at this position
- **Difficulty escalation** — rising ADD across positions
- **Cross-family transition** — composition across movement systems
- **Compositional layering** — the modifier on whirl (blurry) is a layered structure on top of the cross-family entry

### Frequency in corpus
butterfly → blurry whirl: 3 documented appearances. Less common than Example 1 but architecturally informative.

## Example 4 — Food Processor → Mobius (difficulty stacking)

```
food processor  →  mobius
   6 ADD             5 ADD
   = 11 ADD total (no recovery between)
```

### What's happening

Two ultra-high-ADD tricks chained back-to-back with no low-ADD recovery between. The player commits to maximum difficulty across both positions.

### Why this architecture is rare

- **No recovery slot.** Most high-ADD sequences insert a stabilizer (whirl → whirl, legover → legover) between two hard tricks. This sequence skips that.
- **Compound fatigue.** Mobius (gyro torque) demands precise rotational control immediately after food processor (blurry symposium whirl) — a 4-modifier compound. The transition window is tight.
- **One-miss collapse.** This is concentration strategy at its extreme. Either both tricks land or the sequence fails entirely.

### Concepts surfaced
- **Concentration strategy** — extreme version
- **Difficulty stacking** — consecutive ultra-high-ADD with no recovery
- **Sequence risk** — single-miss-collapse
- **Per-trick ADD density** — 5.5 ADD/trick average

### Frequency in corpus
3 documented appearances, all 2004–2016. The architecture exists in the elite tier but doesn't dominate.

## Example 5 — The 22-ADD Solis Chain (breadth via length)

```
butterfly  →  whirl  →  osis  →  dimwalk  →  osis  →  butterfly  →  swirl
  3            3        3        4           3        3            3
= 22 ADD across 7 tricks
```

### What's happening

Greg Solis, 2008. Seven tricks averaging 3.1 ADD each, no modifier compounds, no maximum-ADD elements — pure breadth strategy taken to its corpus-maximum result.

### Why this architecture works

- **Length over depth.** No single trick exceeds 4 ADD; the accumulation comes from sequence length, not from per-trick difficulty.
- **Recovery built in.** Multiple positions through legover-family rest points (osis at positions 3 and 5) let the body reset mid-chain.
- **Rotational anchor.** The chain alternates between butterfly-family and whirl/swirl-family elements, maintaining rotational continuity throughout.
- **Composure as the bottleneck.** The collapse mode here is fatigue or distraction across seven elements; the player's challenge is sustained focus rather than peak technical demand.

### Concepts surfaced
- **Breadth strategy** — corpus-maximum example
- **Sequence length** — 7 tricks vs corpus mean of 2.4
- **Recovery trick** — osis appearing twice as a mid-sequence resetting position
- **Per-trick ADD density** — 3.1 ADD/trick (compare to Kyle Hewitt's concentration architecture at 5.3)
- **Sequence risk** — accumulation-break

### Frequency in corpus
1 documented appearance. The corpus-maximum sequence.

## Pedagogical layering — how to read these examples

A first-time reader sees five vignettes. A more careful reader sees the cross-cutting taxonomy:

| Architecture | Example | Total ADD | Trick count | ADD/trick |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Canonical pair | 1 | 8 | 2 | 4.0 |
| Walking ladder | 2 | 8+ | 3+ | ~3.0 |
| Cross-family launch | 3 | 8+ | 2+ | ~4.0 |
| Difficulty stacking | 4 | 11 | 2 | 5.5 |
| Breadth-via-length | 5 | 22 | 7 | 3.1 |

The columns make the architectural decisions visible. Examples 1 and 3 occupy similar ADD-density space with different cross-family choices; example 5 takes breadth to its corpus-maximum extreme; example 4 takes concentration to its extreme. The vocabulary maps to real architectural choices, not abstract categories.
