## 7. paradox-tutorial.txt — content summary for PM1-PM4 (per curator directive 2026-05-19)

Source: Steve Goldberg, FAQ entry "What does 'paradox' mean?", Advanced Freestyle section. Last updated 2-Jan-1999.

### 7.1 Definition (verbatim from source line 61-65)

> "Paradox" as a term only makes sense when used to describe a move that is more difficult than, but otherwise identical to another move — wherein the difference is entirely attributed to the *set*. Furthermore, the set for a paradox move is always from the cross-body position.

The tutorial frames paradox as BOTH a concept AND a 6th ADD category ("a 6th add category because the 5 existing categories simply aren't sufficient to describe all types of difficulty"). Modern doctrine treats paradox as a +1 body modifier — the tutorial's "6th ADD category" framing is a historical-precursor to the modern +1 multiplier classification.

### 7.2 Worked example (verbatim from source line 40-44)

> `clip > op in dex > op toe` — "mirage" (from clipper set)
> `clip > SAME in dex > op toe` — "paradox mirage"

The same-leg vs opposite-leg distinction at the in-dex segment is the paradox marker. Source description: "instead of setting the exact same left-leg in-out dexterity using your right clipper, you start out with a set from your LEFT clipper. This makes the move APPRECIABLY HARDER."

### 7.3 Why paradox is harder (two reasons, source lines 70-79)

1. **The "snake" motion.** The setting leg must "snake out from the cross-body position quickly and precisely in order to get back under the bag for the initial dexterity."
2. **The "double-hip-pivot".** The body must "pivot hips more than usual to perform the move — first in one direction, then in the other."

The "double-hip-pivot" is named as the structural justification for the +1 body ADD: "in the current difficulty-rating system, a 'paradox' move is awarded an extra 'body' add (for the double-hip-pivot)." This is the 1999 source for the modern paradox(+1) doctrine.

### 7.4 Critical exclusions (source line 66)

The tutorial explicitly excludes 4 moves from being "paradox-eligible":

- butterfly
- switch-over
- leg-over
- swirl

Reason: these moves do NOT *require* the double-hip-pivot to execute; the alternate-side set does not appreciably increase difficulty.

Reference cited: "Paul Munger sent out a reasonably good list of criteria for whether or not a move is paradox."

### 7.5 Paradox / spinning / gyro interaction (source line 82)

> "spinning or gyrating moves are thought of as 'taking the place' of paradox in most cases. It is important that you understand that spins or gyrations don't necessarily 'negate' paradox — they don't 'cancel it out'. They simply 'subsume' it."

Example given: vortex is a "gyrating drifter" (non-paradox), NOT a "gyrating paradox drifter" — "the spin of the gyrating motion is a 'body' motion; once the spin is complete the remaining work is very similar to the work required to hit a drifter from the 'simple' set."

This is the 1999 source for the doctrine that spinning/gyro subsumes paradox in compound contexts (cross-references modern Red pt2 surging doctrine + the structural-difficulty-merging principle).

- **Source author:** Steve Goldberg (2-Jan-1999).
