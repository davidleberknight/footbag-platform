# History evidence — concrete numbers from our own pages (planning only)

Mined from Competition (/freestyle/competition, canonical-results-derived) and Insights
(/freestyle/insights, from a documented archive of 395 Sick3 sequences). Each number is an
anchor for a History section, read as evidence for the thesis (freestyle got harder because
each generation expanded the shared vocabulary), not as a statistic. Verify against the live
pages before publishing; these are current values.

## The growth of the sport (Competition, event history)

Documented freestyle events by decade (singles freestyle; doubles/team excluded):

- 1980s: 23
- 1990s: 49
- 2000s: 144
- 2010s: 41
- 2020s: 13
- Total: 270 documented freestyle events.

Caveat: these are DOCUMENTED events, so the 2000s peak reflects both the competitive high
point and the density of documentation in that era (footbag.org, the archive). Present it as
the sport reaching a documented peak, not as a literal claim that activity later collapsed.

## What became central to the shared language (Insights, most-used)

Most-used tricks across the 395-sequence archive (mentions, players):

- whirl 150, 86 players (far ahead of everything)
- pixie (modifier) 121, 75
- swirl 96, 44
- blurry whirl 89, 53
- torque 78, 49
- butterfly 56, 48; legover 51, 31; mirage 48, 39

The foundational atoms the encyclopedia teaches (whirl, swirl, butterfly, legover, mirage) are
the same tricks the record shows became central. The dictionary's foundations are not an
editorial choice; they are what players actually built on.

## Whirl became a structural hub, not merely popular (Insights, connectors + transitions)

Connector analysis (how many distinct tricks each connects to):

- whirl 99 connections, 86 players (again far ahead)
- blurry whirl 70; ripwalk 61; butterfly 44; torque 39; swirl 34; mirage 30

Most-documented two-trick transitions:

- blurry whirl -> whirl 17 (15 players), the single most documented structure
- ripwalk -> whirl 11; whirl -> whirl 10; torque -> whirl 4

Whirl is both the most-used AND the most-connected trick, and the most common transitions land
on it. This is the Whirl page's claim (it became the connector combos return to) proven from
the record, not asserted.

## The vocabulary expanded by composition, not endless invention (modifier frequency, live)

Modifiers by how many active canonical tricks carry each (top of the live dictionary count):

- symposium 111
- ducking 109
- spinning 103
- paradox 78
- pixie 74; stepping 71; fairy 69; gyro 50

A handful of operators each generate roughly 70 to 110 tricks by layering onto known bases.
The vocabulary grew large not by inventing hundreds of unrelated moves but by composing a small
set of operators onto a small set of foundations, exactly the thesis, quantified.

## The difficulty ceiling rose over time (Insights, notable sequences)

Highest-ADD documented sequences (player, year, ADD, length):

- Greg Solis 2008, 22 ADD, 7 tricks: butterfly > whirl > osis > dimwalk > osis > butterfly > swirl
- Stefan Siegert 2005, 18; Cody Rushing 2008, 18; Daniel Cadavid 2021, 18
- a cluster of 15-16 ADD sequences spanning 2002 to 2013

The record of hardest sequences runs from the early 2000s into the 2020s; difficulty is a moving
ceiling players raised generation over generation, the thesis in its plainest form.

## Breadth of vocabulary per player (Insights, diverse players)

Players by unique tricks landed in the archive:
