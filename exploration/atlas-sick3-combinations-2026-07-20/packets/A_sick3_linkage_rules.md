# Review packet A: did Sick 3 tricks have to link directly?

Self-contained. Question owner: Red / curator (historical rules). Nothing in production
was changed; the two affected tricks (bedwetter, pixie paradon) are frozen until this and
packet-specific questions resolve.

## The question

In historical Sick 3 contests, did the three tricks have to link directly (the delay that
ends one trick IS the set of the next), or could a performer insert an ordinary setup
contact (a plain toe or clipper set) between tricks without invalidating the "3"?

## Why it matters

The Atlas compatibility model tests direct-delay chaining. Under it, 110 of 117
fully-resolved historical sequences (94%) chain cleanly; 5 do not. If bridging contacts
were legal, "apparently incompatible" stops being evidence about trick notation at all.
If direct linking was required, those 5 sequences are live evidence that two dictionary
entries are notated too narrowly (see packet-internal note below).

## Historical rule-language found (all from mirror event pages; no formal IFPA rule text located)

1. "Sick 3 - Best combination of three tricks you can string together"
   (mirror event page, legacy id 1200325415)
2. "Sick 3 = Basically, whoever hits the phattest 3 trick comb (out of ten tries)"
   (mirror event page, legacy id 1007420059)
3. "Sick 3 (Hit combos in italics ...)" — results distinguishing HIT combos from attempts
   (mirror event page, legacy id 1163784775)
4. "OPEN SICK 3-TRICK COMBO" / "Sick 3 Trick Combo" — the format is consistently called a
   combo, never a medley or a routine.
5. No page found that states "in a row", "without a drop", or any explicit
   intermediate-contact rule. The IFPA rules pages in the mirror do not mention Sick 3.

## Statistical evidence from the corpus itself

- 94% of resolved sequences chain directly (surface-to-surface) with zero bridging
  assumed. If free bridging had been common practice, there would be no reason for
  performed sequences to cluster so overwhelmingly on direct-linkable transitions.
- 4 of the 5 non-chaining sequences fail at the SAME trick (bedwetter, entered from a
  clipper delay in four independent performances across 2004-2019). A single recurring
  trick is better explained by that trick having a clipper-entry form the dictionary does
  not carry than by a general bridging allowance.

## Recommended answer

Treat "combo / string together / hit" as meaning DIRECT linking (the community-vernacular
sense), adopt direct linking as the model default, and resolve the bedwetter and pixie
paradon cases as trick-level entry questions (does bedwetter have a clipper-entry form?
was pixie paradon performed from a toe set?) rather than by weakening the linkage rule.
A bare confirmation from Red on the vernacular meaning of "combo" in Sick 3 context
settles this packet.

## Impact when ruled

- Ruling "direct linking" (recommended): compatibility statistics stand as computed; the
  5 incompatible sequences become live notation evidence feeding the bedwetter /
  pixie-paradon entry questions.
- Ruling "bridging allowed": the two link-status columns lose evidential force against
  notation; statistics would be re-labeled (compatibility becomes "direct-linkable"
  descriptive, not normative). No catalog rows change either way.
