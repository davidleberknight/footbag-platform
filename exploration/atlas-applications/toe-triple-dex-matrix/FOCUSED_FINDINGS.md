# Focused Findings

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. Direct answers to the five questions the slice poses, from `triple_dex_matrix.csv`, `distinct_formula_catalog.csv`, and the reports in this folder. No realizability, naming, or promotion claim.

## 1. Why do Pixie Same-Side Eggbeater and Pixie Same-Side Double Leg Over collapse into their ordinary variants?

Because "same-side" is here a redundant terminal-receiver label, not a structural change. Both ordinary tricks already land on the near (same) foot: Pixie Eggbeater is `TOE > SAME IN > OP OUT > OP OUT > SAME TOE` and Pixie Double Leg Over is `TOE > SAME IN > OP IN > OP OUT > SAME TOE`, and both end `SAME TOE`. A "same-side" reading names that already-present near terminal, so it produces the identical formula and collapses onto the ordinary version.

The Atlas confirms this is the terminal-receiver reading and not a leg-side reading, because the leg-side reading fails. Had "same-side" flipped the eggbeater's two OP dexes to SAME, it would have defined a different base (`SAME IN, SAME OUT, SAME OUT`), a distinct unnamed formula, which would not have collapsed. The collapse happened, so the label was redundant terminal wording. The two positional axes give two predictions and only one matches the observed collapse.

## 2. Does the same structural base admit a distinct FAR-side variant?

Yes. For each of the two bases the far (opposite-foot) terminal is a different notation and a genuinely distinct, representable formula with no active occupant:

- Far Pixie Eggbeater: `TOE > SAME IN > OP OUT > OP OUT > OP TOE` (distinct, unnamed).
- Far Pixie Double Leg Over: `TOE > SAME IN > OP IN > OP OUT > OP TOE` (distinct, unnamed).

These are class D coordinates, expressible and unoccupied. This records only that the coordinate exists and is empty; it makes no claim that a body can perform it and proposes no name.

## 3. Which other triple-dex bases show the same pattern?

The pattern, near terminal named and far terminal an empty distinct coordinate, holds for thirteen of the sixty-four bases. Beyond the two Pixie bases, they include the fairy, quantum, tapping, atomic, and sailing-entry chains and the pure triples, for example:

- `TOE > SAME OUT > OP OUT > OP OUT > SAME TOE` (named fairy_eggbeater) with far `... > OP TOE` empty.
- `TOE > OP IN > OP OUT > OP OUT > SAME TOE` (named bedwetter, quantum_eggbeater) with far empty.
- `TOE > OP OUT > OP OUT > OP OUT > SAME TOE` (named bladerunner, tapping_eggbeater, atomic_eggbeater) with far empty.
- `TOE > SAME OUT > SAME OUT > SAME OUT > SAME TOE` (named triple_orbit) with far empty.

The full list of thirteen is in `OCCUPANCY_SUMMARY.md`. The same shape recurs at every named near-terminal base except the two barrage-family bases, which are named at the far terminal instead.

## 4. Does the current dictionary systematically omit FAR variants, or only fail to name them?

It fails to name them; it does not fail to express them. Every far counterpart of a named near form is a class D formula, distinct and fully representable in the current notation, never a class E representation-ambiguous one. The omission is lexical, not structural: the movement language can write `OP TOE` as readily as `SAME TOE`, and the corpus does so for the barrage family, so the far coordinates are reachable and simply unlabeled.

The counts make the asymmetry concrete. Of the seventeen bases that carry any name, thirteen name only the near terminal, two name only the far, and two name both. The dictionary has a strong near-terminal naming bias and leaves the matching far coordinates empty but available. Two bases show the distinction being lexicalized when the community did name both sides: base `SIN-OIN-SIN` is pixie_double_pickup at the near terminal and pixie_barrage at the far, one base, two named tricks, two terminals.

## 5. Can the Atlas reliably expose these variants without relying on historical naming?

Yes, for the same/op terminal distinction, which is what this slice needed. The matrix was generated purely from the leg-side and terminal structure, with no name attached until the final corpus comparison, and it surfaced all 128 distinct formulas, marked which are occupied, and identified the empty far coordinates for every named near form, entirely from structure. Formula identity, not name matching, also revealed the seven formulas that one movement wears under several names, a relationship literal-name comparison would have missed.

The one honest limit is the receiver-distance nuance. The Atlas reliably exposes which foot the terminal lands on, near or far, because the notation writes that. It cannot expose a finer near/far distance beyond that foot, because the notation does not preserve it; those readings are representation-ambiguous. So the Atlas answers the terminal-side question reliably and name-independently, and correctly declines the sub-question the representation cannot support.

## One-line conclusion

The Atlas, run as a tool, both explained the false Pixie same-side distinctions (redundant near-terminal wording) and exposed their real structural counterparts (empty, representable far coordinates), and it showed the dictionary's near-terminal naming bias is a naming gap, not a representational one, all from structure without leaning on historical names.
