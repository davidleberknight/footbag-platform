## Direction
**Core Concept · connective** *(the "direction is structural" insight is homed at Mirage, not here)*

**Line.** A dexterity travels one of two ways around the leg — inward or outward. The
direction is set the instant the foot starts the bag moving, and it is one of the two
choices that make one dex a different move from another.

**Relates.** Direction pairs with side: together they are what distinguish the two-point
atoms from each other (see Side). Reversing a dex's direction does not give you a softer or
harder version of the same trick — it gives you a *different* trick, with its own name,
trained and scored on its own terms. That is the lesson mirage and illusion teach (mirage
one way, illusion the other), and it is the heart of the Mirror Law. Every atom has a
direction mirror: around the world / orbit, mirage / illusion, whirl / whip.

**Explore.** Side (the dex's other axis) · Mirage and Illusion (a direction pair) ·
Dexterity (the unit direction applies to) · essay: The Mirror Law.

## Side
**Core Concept · insight home: side is structural**

**Line.** A dexterity works either on your own side — the side of the leg you are standing
on — or crosses to the opposite side of the body. Same-side and opposite-side describe
where the bag travels relative to your plant leg. Around the world stays on its own side;
mirage crosses to the far side.

**Relates.** Side is the second of the two choices that define a dex, alongside direction
(see Direction). The four two-point atoms lay both axes out at once: around the world and
orbit are same-side, mirage and illusion are opposite-side. Which side the bag travels to
is not a matter of style or reach — like direction, it is part of what the trick *is*.

**Reveals.** Two tricks can share everything the eye notices — the same landing, the same
difficulty, even the same direction of travel — and still be two different tricks, because
one works on your own side and the other crosses the body. Side is a full structural axis,
not a variation on a theme. Around the world and mirage are not "the same trick done
wider"; they are different atoms because the bag goes to different sides. Once you hold
direction and side as two independent switches, the vocabulary's crowd of near-identical
pairs stops being confusing: each atom can be flipped on either switch, and each flip is a
real, separately named, separately scored trick. Half the dictionary is the other half
with one switch thrown.

**Explore.** Direction (the other axis) · Around the World (a same-side atom) · Mirage (an
opposite-side atom) · Dexterity · essay: The Mirror Law (where direction and side are
assembled).

## Surface Frame
**Core Concept · insight home: surface frame changes meaning**

> **Status: prototype.** Public rendering of this entry stays gated until the reverse-swirl
> notation question is settled (a curator/doctrine verification item). The Reveal below is
> written to the confirmed structural claim only; it makes no reverse-swirl claim and
> asserts no exact around-the-world / orbit identity for swirl.

**Line.** The same dexterity can mean two different things depending on the surface it is
framed by — the surface it is set from and the surface it is caught on. A dex read onto a
toe stall and the "same" dex read across the body onto a clipper are not one trick; the
frame is part of the identity.

**Relates.** Surface frame is a third structural axis, beside direction and side (see
Direction, Side) — and a genuinely separate one, not those two renamed. Its cleanest
worked example is the clipper world: whirl is mirage read in a clipper frame — the same
opposite-side inward dex, caught cross-body on the clipper instead of on the toe. Swirl is
usefully pictured as the clipper world's around-the-world, but only loosely — a teaching
analogy, not a mechanical copy. **The family guard:** an analogy across surfaces helps you
learn, but it never overrides family identity. A reversed-direction trick is its own family
or lineage, not a member of the forward trick's family — reverse swirl is not in the swirl
family, orbit is not in the around-the-world family, and whip is not in the whirl family.
Surface analogy does not merge those lineages.

**Reveals.** A dex does not carry a fixed meaning that you transfer from one surface to the
next. Its meaning is re-derived inside the frame it is performed in — the set surface, the
plant leg, the catching surface, and whether it stays on your side or crosses the body.
This is why whirl is its own trick and not "a kind of mirage": same dex, different frame,
different identity. The analogy a beginner reaches for is usually the wrong one, because it
transfers meaning that has to be re-earned in the new frame. Learn to read the frame
first, and a whole layer of tricks that look nearly identical falls cleanly apart.

*Authoring note (not reader-facing): the load-bearing structural fact under the swirl-type
endings is that the dexing leg is also the catching leg; the toe↔clipper mappings are
verified, but the stored reverse-swirl notation remains a curator verification item and is
not to be rewritten. Keep this entry's public prose parked until that settles.*

**Explore.** Direction · Side (its sibling axes) · Whirl (the clean worked example) ·
Mirage (whirl's toe-frame twin) · essay: The Mirror Law.

## Set vs Operator
**Core Concept · insight home: sets vs operators is a question of role, not mechanic**

**Line.** The same movement can play two jobs. When it launches a trick — puts the bag up
and starts the sequence — it is a *set*. When it modifies a trick already under way, it is
an *operator*. Set or operator is a question of the job the movement is doing, not of the
movement itself.

**Relates.** This distinction is the hinge between the vocabulary's atoms and its
compounds: a trick is a base, often launched by a set, with operators laid over it (see
Composition). The clearest case is barrage and furious — the very same two-dex structure is
called a barrage when it is the trick you perform and furious when it is a modifier applied
to another trick. One mechanic, two names, because it is doing two different jobs.

**Reveals.** Whether a movement is a set or an operator is not written into the movement —
it is decided by where the movement sits in the trick. The same two-dex burst is a set in
one trick and an operator in another. So you cannot sort freestyle's vocabulary by looking
at movements in isolation; you have to ask what job each piece is doing in the trick it
appears in. Once role comes apart from mechanic, the operator layer stops looking like a
second pile of moves to memorize and becomes a small set of *jobs* that the atoms you
already know can be assigned to.

**Explore.** Composition (how bases and operators combine) · Barrage and Furious (one
structure, two roles) · the operators (miraging, whirling, stepping, paradox) · Dexterity.

## Composition
**Core Concept · connective** *(the payoff insight — the algebra — is homed at Blur, Torque, and Butterfly, not here)*

**Line.** A freestyle trick is usually not one indivisible thing — it is a structure: a
base move with operators added to it. "Miraging osis," "blurry mirage," "two dexes in one
set" are all compositions: a foundation plus what has been layered on. To read a trick is
to read its parts.

**Relates.** Composition is the premise that makes the whole encyclopedia legible. A base
(see Core Atoms) plus one or more operators (see Set vs Operator) gives a named compound,
and the count of parts is exactly what the trick scores (see ADD). This is the idea that
lets torque be read as "miraging osis" and blur as "blurry mirage" rather than memorized
whole. The revelation this premise leads to — that famous short names are secretly formulas
— is the small algebra the encyclopedia is built around; it lands at Blur, Torque, and
Butterfly (see Name vs Structure), and is deliberately not pre-fired here.

**Explore.** Core Atoms (the bases) · Set vs Operator (the roles that combine) · ADD (why
the parts are the score) · Torque and Blur (compositions wearing famous names) · essay:
Name vs Structure.

## Core Atoms (the twelve)
**Core Concept · insight home: every trick terminates in one of twelve core atoms**

**Line.** Under freestyle's sprawling vocabulary sits a small, fixed set of core atoms —
the irreducible moves that cannot be broken down into anything simpler. However elaborate a
trick's name, it decomposes to one of them. Around the world, mirage, whirl, butterfly, and
osis are atoms; torque and blur are not — they are atoms with operators added.

**Relates.** The core atoms are the *closure set* of the vocabulary — the fixed alphabet
every trick is spelled from — not a ranking of the best or most-performed tricks. They are
the landing points every dex resolves onto: the two resting stalls (toe and clipper) and
the single-dex atoms that reach them — around the world / orbit, mirage / illusion, legover
/ pickup, whirl / swirl, butterfly, and osis. Compounds are built by adding operators to
these (see Composition); families grow by extending one of them (see Family).

**Reveals.** The number of core atoms is small and fixed — about a dozen. This is the quiet
fact that makes freestyle learnable rather than endless: the hundreds of named tricks are
not hundreds of independent things to memorize, they are combinations drawn from a short
alphabet. Learn the atoms and the handful of operators, and most of the dictionary becomes
*readable* instead of memorizable — you can meet a trick you have never seen, decompose it
to its atom, and know what it is. A closed alphabet under an open vocabulary is the most
reassuring thing the encyclopedia can show a learner, and it is the opposite of a "top
tricks" list: these are not the tricks that matter most, they are the ones everything else
is made of.

**Explore.** Dexterity (the unit that lands on an atom) · Mirage, Whirl, Butterfly, Osis
(atoms with their own entries) · Composition (how atoms become compounds) · essay: Name vs
Structure.

## ADD (additive scoring), and the notation checksum
**Core Concept · insight home: the bracket count equals the ADD**

> **Spine note.** ADD-the-idea is taught early (the scoring section) and needs no notation.
> The Reveal below — the self-checking bracket count — needs the written notation to land,
> so it belongs *late* in the spine, with the notation section (registry release V1.2). The
> two are one insight home split across two positions; do not front-load the notation to
> deliver the Reveal early.

**Line.** ADD — "added difficulty" — is freestyle's difficulty score, and it is
refreshingly literal: it counts the structural parts of a trick. One point for a dex,
another for a second dex, another for each operator laid on. A bare stall is one point; a
plain two-point atom is a dex landing on a stall; a barrage, with two dexes in one set, is
three.

**Relates.** Because ADD is just a count of parts, it follows straight from composition
(see Composition): read a trick's structure and you can read its score. This is why the
encyclopedia can call torque "miraging osis" and know what it scores — the parts are the
points. The same additive logic is what the notation, taught later, turns into something
you can check for yourself.

**Reveals.** Once freestyle is written down, the score checks itself. In the notation each
scoring part of a trick sits in its own bracket, and the number of brackets *is* the ADD —
so a reader can count the brackets and confirm the number is honest, trusting no one. A
scoring system that audits its own arithmetic in front of you is a rare thing, and it is
the strongest possible answer to "how do you know that trick is worth that much?" — you do
not have to know, you can see it. This is also why the notation is taught late rather than
early: it is most powerful once you already understand what it is counting.

**Explore.** Composition (why the parts are the score) · Barrage (two dexes, three points)
· the notation (where the checksum lives) · the frontier (the scoring ceiling).

**Why two carried no Reveal — the two most important calls here.**

- **Direction is connective, not a home.** The registry deliberately homes "direction is
  structural" at Mirage — the concrete trick where a player *feels* the reversal — not at
  the abstract Direction concept. Giving Direction its own Reveal would duplicate Mirage's
  and stack two geometry Reveals back to back. Direction carries the one-line reference in
  Relates instead. This is what keeps the geometry axes spaced.
- **Composition earns no Reveal.** It is the premise the algebra insight is built on. Its
  payoff — "famous short names are secretly formulas" — is the collapsed algebra staircase
  homed at Blur → Torque → Butterfly. Revealing it at Composition would pre-fire Blur's aha
  and dull it three entries early, the exact failure the algebra-collapse rule guards
  against. Composition prepares the reader for Torque, Blender, Blur, and the Name-vs-
  Structure essay precisely by *setting up* the reveal, not spending it.

**On the density.** Five Reveals across seven entries is far denser than the pilot's eight
across fifteen — but this is expected and not Reveal inflation. Core Concepts *are* the
graph's hubs, and the registry already designates rows 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 as homed at these
very entries. Every Reveal here is a registered insight authored at its canonical home; no
new insight was invented, and the count stays at eleven. The safeguard that matters for
this tranche is not fewer Reveals but **pacing**: these homes must be spread across the
spine (the three geometry Reveals especially — Mirage, then Side, then Surface Frame — never
consecutive), and Set-vs-Operator, Core Atoms, and ADD already fall in different spine
sections (grammar, vocabulary, scoring).

**Surface Frame is authored but held.** Per the standing constraint, its public prose stays
gated until the reverse-swirl notation question settles. The Reveal is written to the
confirmed structural claim only, carries the family guard (surface analogy does not override
family identity), and makes no reverse-swirl or exact ATW/orbit-identity claim. The
load-bearing fact (dexing leg is the catching leg) is kept in an authoring note, not in
reader-facing prose.
