# Glossary V2 — Pilot Entries, Tranche 2 (the operator/unit prerequisites)

Prototypes, not production. Purpose: complete the prerequisite path so **Torque**
(miraging osis), **Blender** (whirling osis), and **Blur** (blurry mirage) can be
explained without undefined terms. Seven entries — the fundamental unit, one core atom,
and five operators. Written in the pilot voice; structural facts checked against the
live dictionary (read-only; no data changed). Reveals are rare and none is claimed here
(see the review at the end for why that is the correct result). No public Surface Frame
prose is written — that axis remains under notation verification.

Kinds used: **Core Concept** (a graph hub, an abstract idea), **Foundational Physical
Skill** (a learner gateway trick), **Operator** (a modifier applied to a base). These
are kept distinct per the rulings.

---

## Dexterity (dex)
**Core Concept · connective**

**Line.** A dexterity — a *dex* — is the fundamental unit of freestyle movement: one
controlled trip of the bag around or across the leg, from a resting stall and back to
one. Almost every trick is one or more dexes strung together, and when people say a
trick is "busy" or count how much is happening, they are counting its dexes.

**Relates.** A dex always has a direction (inward or outward) and a side (your own side,
or across to the opposite side); those two choices are what make one dex a *different
trick* from another (see Direction, Side). A dex begins and ends on a stall, which is
why a stall is an anchor state and a trick is the journey between anchors (see Toe
Stall). And a finished dex lands on one of the twelve core atoms — the dex is how you
travel, the atom is where you arrive.

**Explore.** Direction · Side · Toe Stall (where a dex begins and ends) · the twelve
core atoms (where a dex lands) · Mirage (the simplest single-dex trick).

---

## Osis
**Foundational Physical Skill · core atom · connective**

**Line.** An osis is one of the twelve core atoms: the body turns and the bag is caught
cross-body on the clipper — a rotational move that lands *behind* the leg rather than in
front of you. You will return to this one constantly, because an entire line of famous
tricks is built directly on top of it.

**Relates.** Osis is a rotational, cross-body atom — a cousin of whirl, swirl, and
butterfly, which also land cross-body on the clipper. What makes it worth returning to
is what gets built on it: add a single operator to an osis and you get a famous-sounding
trick. Torque is miraging osis; blender is whirling osis. Both *feel* like fundamentals,
but each is really an osis plus one operator (see Torque, and Name vs Structure).

**Explore.** The twelve core atoms · Torque (miraging osis) · Blender (whirling osis) ·
Whirl and Swirl (its fellow cross-body atoms) · Miraging and Whirling (the operators
that extend it).

---

## Miraging
**Operator · connective**

**Line.** Miraging adds an inward, opposite-side dexterity to the front of a trick, for
one added point of difficulty. It is the operator that turns a plain atom into a named
compound — most famously, miraging an osis gives a torque.

**Relates.** Miraging is the inward-traveling operator; you can picture it as the inward
counterpart of atomic, which travels outward. That mirror is only an aid to picturing
the motion, not a claim that either operator is built from the other. Applied to other
atoms it yields other named tricks: miraging a clipper gives a drifter, miraging a
legover gives a double-leg-over. Its near-twin is whirling, which adds a similar inward
dex but sends the trick's landing to the other side — the exact difference between
torque and blender.

**Explore.** Torque (miraging osis) · Osis (its most important base) · Whirling (its
near-twin) · Drifter (miraging clipper) · Mirage (the atom it is named from).

---

## Whirling
**Operator · connective**

**Line.** Whirling adds an inward dexterity along a whirl path to the front of a trick,
for one added point of difficulty. Whirling an osis gives a blender.

**Relates.** Whirling is miraging's near-twin: both add an inward opposite-side dex, but
whirling follows the whirl path, which sends the trick's cross-body landing to the *same*
side instead of the opposite side. That single difference is exactly what separates
blender (whirling osis, same-side landing) from torque (miraging osis, opposite-side
landing) — two famous tricks, one shared base, told apart by which operator drove the
dex. Whirling appears on other bases too, such as whirling swirl.

**Explore.** Blender (whirling osis) · Torque (the contrast that shows the difference) ·
Osis (their shared base) · Miraging (its near-twin).

---

## Stepping
**Operator · connective**

**Line.** Stepping adds an inward dexterity as a fresh clipper entry at the very front
of a trick, for one added point of difficulty. It is a set-style move — a quick foot
relocation that launches the rest of the trick — rather than a body twist layered on
top.

**Relates.** Stepping is one of the two operators hidden inside "blurry"; the other is
paradox. On its own it prepends a clipper entry to a base, so a stepping trick begins
one dex earlier than the trick it is built from. It belongs with the other set-style
operators, which launch rather than modify (see Set vs Operator).

**Explore.** Blurry (stepping plus paradox) · Blur (blurry mirage, where stepping's role
appears) · Paradox (stepping's partner inside blurry) · Set vs Operator.

---

## Paradox
**Operator · connective**

**Line.** Paradox adds one point of difficulty by placing a hip-pivot between two
dexterities on the same set, changing the side the second dex works on. It is a
timing-and-orientation relationship between dexes, not a move you can perform on its
own.

**Relates.** Paradox is the second operator hidden inside "blurry" (stepping is the
first). Applied directly it names tricks like paradox mirage (three points: mirage's two
plus paradox's one). One honest caveat: whether a paradox term always scores, across
every naming lineage, is one of the open questions the encyclopedia has flagged for the
rules expert — so read a paradox as settled only where the dictionary already scores it.

**Explore.** Blurry (stepping plus paradox) · Paradox Mirage (paradox on an atom) ·
Stepping (its partner inside blurry) · the frontier (where the open scoring questions
live).

---

## Blurry
**Operator (composite) · connective**

**Line.** Blurry is folk shorthand for a stepping-based operator — not a single fixed
value. In some named tricks it resolves as stepping alone; in others as stepping plus
paradox. It is the operator that turns a mirage into a blur.

**Relates.** Blurry is the first place a *name* hides a small formula, but the formula is
not fixed: blur is blurry mirage, which unfolds to stepping + paradox + mirage, while
ripwalk is blurry butterfly, where blurry is stepping alone (see Blur, and Name vs
Structure). So the reader should always read the named trick's own decomposition rather
than adding a fixed "blurry" value — the label compresses a stepping-based stack, and which
stack depends on the trick. The exact weight of "blurry" in isolation is a doctrine detail,
not a settled single number.

**Explore.** Blur (blurry mirage — the front-door case of a name hiding a formula) ·
Stepping and Paradox (the two operators inside it) · Mirage (the atom it modifies) ·
Name vs Structure (the essay it feeds).

# Review

Short assessment of the tranche against its purpose (make Torque, Blender, and Blur
explicable without undefined terms), read adversarially against the architecture.

## Which entries worked

All seven read cleanly in the Line/Relates/Explore shape. **Dexterity, Osis, Miraging,
Whirling, and Stepping** are unproblematic. **Paradox** works but carries a stated
open-doctrine caveat (does a paradox term always score). **Blurry** works and is the
most valuable entry of the seven — see below.

## Which lacked prerequisites

None lacked a *tranche-internal* prerequisite. But the entries reference forward to
**Core Concept entries that are not yet written** — Direction, Side, Set vs Operator,
and a "twelve core atoms" entry. Those are Relates links, not blockers, but they are the
next real prerequisite gap: the operator layer is now defined, and the abstract-concept
layer beneath it (direction, side, set/operator, composition) is the batch that should
follow to make the whole lower half of the dependency graph self-contained.

## Does the tranche complete the Torque/Blender/Blur path?

- **Torque = miraging osis → complete.** Both terms are now defined; the entry can be
  authored with no undefined vocabulary.
- **Blender = whirling osis → complete.** Same. The miraging/whirling pair also gives
  Torque and Blender their "one shared base, told apart by one operator" contrast for
  free.
- **Blur = blurry mirage → complete, with one honest caveat.** Blur unfolds to
  stepping + paradox + mirage and sums to four, so it is teachable now. But authoring
  Blurry surfaced a real dictionary ambiguity: **"blurry" is stored as +1 in the modifier
  registry yet reads as +2 (stepping + paradox) inside blur and its siblings.** Blur the
  *trick* decomposes cleanly; "blurry" the *operator* has two recorded weights. The Blur
  entry (and the Blurry entry) must carry that caveat rather than assert a single number.

## Which Reveals earned their place

**None — and that is the correct, expected result, not a gap.** Every entry in this
tranche is a Core Concept, a Foundational Physical Skill, or an operator, and each
*references* an existing insight in its Relates layer (Dexterity → the stall and
core-atom insights; Osis and Blurry → the algebra insight; Paradox → the open frontier)
without homing one. This validates the architecture's "Reveals are rare" rule from the
other direction: the operator/unit layer is the *plumbing that enables* the
compressed-formula revelations at Torque, Blur, and Butterfly — it must not compete with
them. A tranche of prerequisites with zero Reveals is the design working as intended.

## Whether any insight-registry changes are needed

**No new insight, no merge, no split — the eleven-insight canon holds.** Two items to
record, neither a registry-structure change:

1. **The blurry +1/+2 ambiguity is a doctrine-verification item** of the same kind as the
   `rev_swirl` notation question: the dictionary carries two readings, and the glossary
   must teach the caveat rather than pick a side. It touches insight 5 (the algebra) at
   its Blur home — blur's "name hides a formula" is true, but "blurry" itself is one of
   the places the formula is not a single clean number. Flag it for the curator; do not
   resolve it here.
2. **Surface Frame prose was deliberately not written** (per instruction). Whirling's
   entry describes the same-side vs opposite-side *landing* of blender vs torque, which is
   a plain notation fact about those two tricks, not a surface-frame teaching claim; the
   surface-frame axis (toe vs clipper context, whirl≈clipper-mirage, and swirl and reverse
   swirl as directionally-related but distinct lineages, not one family) stays out of
   public prose until its notation and family verification is settled.
3. **Family guard (per the correction that prompted this note):** direction reversal
   generates a distinct family/lineage — reverse swirl is not in the swirl family, orbit
   is not in the ATW family, whip is not in the whirl family — and surface-frame analogy
   does not override family identity. None of the tranche-2 entries claim otherwise (the
   operator and atom entries make no direction-reversal family claims), but authors of the
   forthcoming compound and surface-frame entries must hold this rule.

## Recommended next step

Author the **Core Concept batch** (Direction, Side, Set vs Operator, Composition, and the
twelve-core-atoms entry) so the forward references in this tranche resolve, then the
compound entries (Torque, Blender) — which can now be written against a complete
prerequisite path. Blur waits on a curator note settling how to present the blurry
weight.
