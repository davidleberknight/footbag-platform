# Comparison with Historical Practice

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. It compares the structural taxonomy discovered in `VOLUME_IV.md` with the freestyle vocabulary the dictionary already carries, as observation only, after the structural taxonomy was fixed. It is not criticism, not a promotion or naming proposal, and it changes nothing. Evidence: the named-family field of the 395-movement real corpus, cross-tabulated against the eight structural signatures.

## The method, kept honest

The eight structural families were derived from the dexterity-pair relation alone, before any name was consulted. Only then was each real two-dex movement's historical family label read and cross-tabulated against its structural signature. The comparison asks three questions: which named families line up with a structural family, which mix several, and which structural families the vocabulary has never named.

## The result: the two are orthogonal axes

The cross-tabulation is many-to-many, decisively:

- **Each named family spans many structural signatures.** The largest named families spread across most or all eight: the whirl family spans all eight structural signatures, osis and inside-stall span seven, pickup six, and mirage, butterfly, swirl, torque, and legover span five each. No named family is confined to one structural signature.
- **Each structural signature contains many named families.** The same-leg-parallel-preserved signature contains twenty distinct named families; same-leg-parallel-changed contains nineteen; the others contain eleven to seventeen. No structural signature is one named family.

A one-to-one correspondence would show each named family in a single signature and each signature in a single named family. The data show the opposite at both ends. The named families and the structural signatures are not two views of one partition; they are two orthogonal axes over the same universe.

## Why they are orthogonal

The reason is visible in what each axis measures. The structural taxonomy classifies by the relation between the two dexterities: same or alternating leg, parallel or reversed direction, preserved or changed receiver. The historical vocabulary classifies by the terminal mechanic, the landing that ends the movement: a whirl, a mirage, a butterfly, an osis are named for how the movement finishes, not for how its two dexterities relate. Terminal mechanic and dexterity-pair relation are independent, so a single terminal (say, a whirl landing) is reached by movements of every dexterity-pair relation, and a single dexterity-pair relation ends on many different terminals. The two classifications are orthogonal because they measure orthogonal things.

## The three questions, answered

- **Which historical families correspond closely to a structural family?** None. Every named family spans multiple structural signatures, so no historical family is a structural family in disguise.
- **Which historical families mix several structural families?** All of them, and the largest mix all eight. Mixing is not the exception; it is universal, because the naming axis is orthogonal to the structural one.
- **Which structural families have never been recognized by name?** The dexterity-pair relations themselves. Freestyle names terminals, so the leg-usage, directional-relation, and receiver-behavior distinctions that define the eight structural families are not the axis the vocabulary tracks. The structural families are, as a classification axis, unnamed: real movements populate all eight, but the community's names do not carve along their boundaries.

## What the comparison shows

Freestyle's historical naming and the atlas's structural taxonomy are both real and both natural, and they are different axes. The vocabulary is a terminal-mechanic taxonomy; the atlas discovered a dexterity-pair-relation taxonomy; and the two run orthogonally across the same continuous universe. This is not a disagreement to resolve. It is the empirical form of Volume IV's conclusion: the movement universe carries several independent natural axes, and freestyle's names happen to track one of them (the terminal), while the atlas's facets track others (the dexterity relation and, latently, the carriage). Neither is the taxonomy; both are axes of a lattice.
