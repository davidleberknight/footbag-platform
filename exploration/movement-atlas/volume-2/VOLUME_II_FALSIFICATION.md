# Volume II Falsification Study: is the two-dex universe really the six-cube?

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. An adversarial structural audit of Volume II's central claim, conducted from the movement grammar alone: no trick names, no operator proper-names, no video, no biomechanical appeal beyond what the grammar itself asserts. Per the atlas freeze-and-errata discipline this does not rewrite the frozen Volume II; it records whatever downgrade the evidence forces, as an erratum to it.

The instruction was to assume the six-cube is wrong and to search systematically for every structural reason two dexterities might fail to compose inside a single movement. This study does that, and it succeeds: **the strict claim is false, and the honest structure is weaker than a six-cube.**

## 1. The claim under test, stated precisely

Volume II asserted: over a fixed surface fiber, the set of well-formed two-dexterity movements is the unrestricted Cartesian product of two one-dexterity cubes,

  {two-dex movements} = Cube × Cube, |Cube × Cube| = 8 × 8 = 64,

with the second cube's three axes (side, direction, output side) free and independent of the first. "Unrestricted Cartesian" carries three hidden commitments, and each is a separate target: (i) **completeness** of the vertex set (all 64 well-formed), (ii) **separability** (each coordinate's properties are the pair of its two cube vertices' properties, with no cross-term), and (iii) **flatness** (the second factor is coordinatized the same way regardless of the first, so it is a product and not a twisted bundle). Falsifying any one downgrades the claim.

## 2. Method: what a dexterity consumes and produces

A dexterity, in the grammar, is one leg circling the bag. That definition already fixes the state a dexterity requires to begin and the state it leaves, and every composition failure must live in the mismatch between the two. A single dexterity therefore consumes:

- a **bag position** the circling leg can reach (a carriage state),
- a **free (circling) leg** and a **support leg**, which is a stance parity,
- a **frame** against which its side is same or opposite (a reference foot),

and produces a new carriage state, a new stance, and possibly a new frame. Composition of dex1 then dex2 is well-formed only when everything dex2 consumes is something dex1 can produce. The six-cube claim is exactly the assertion that the only state passed is the carriage side, that it is two-valued, and that dex2 can consume any value of it in any of its eight forms. The audit is the search for a produced-versus-consumed component the grammar recognizes and the six-cube ignored.

## 3. What the grammar specifies about the handoff, and what it omits

The grammar writes a two-dex movement as a launch token, two dexterity tokens each carrying a side and a direction, and one terminal. It records **no explicit intermediate**: between the two dexterity tokens there is no contact, no surface, and no state token at all. The terminal records only the final side. So the grammar **specifies** the carriage side at the ends and **omits** everything about the middle: the intermediate carriage is inferred, the stance parity is never tokenized, the frame's invariance is never asserted, and the bag's height and rotational phase are outside the token set entirely.

This omission is the crux. The six-cube was built by treating the omitted middle as if it were fully captured by a single two-valued side. That is an assumption the grammar permits but does not prove, and Sections 4 through 8 are the places it can fail.

## 4. The falsification axes

Each axis names a produced-versus-consumed component, states the grammar basis, and gives a verdict: **survives** (no reduction), **twists** (product becomes a bundle), **entangles** (separability fails), **prunes** (completeness fails), or **re-categorizes** (the space is not homogeneous).

### Axis A — Carriage-side continuity (the one Volume II modeled)

Dex2's input carriage side is dex1's output. This is the thread Volume II built on. Taken alone, and if side is absolute, a dexterity can begin from either carriage side, so nothing is forbidden. **Verdict: survives, but only conditionally** — its freedom is exactly what Axes B, C, and D put in doubt.

### Axis B — Frame transport (the flatness target)

The grammar fixes a dexterity's side against a reference foot but never asserts that the reference foot is the same one across a two-dex chain. A dexterity that changes which foot sets or supports the bag rotates the frame, and then dex2's side token is measured in a frame that dex1 turned. If so, the second cube's side-axis is re-coordinatized by the first dex, and the space is a **cube-bundle over the first cube with a monodromy**, not a Cartesian product. The cardinality can still be 64, but "unrestricted Cartesian" is the wrong description; the second factor is not independent of the first, it is the first's frame acting on a cube. The grammar neither guarantees frame invariance (which would save flatness) nor forbids it. **Verdict: twists.** Flatness is not grammar-warranted.

### Axis C — Stance parity and same-leg availability (a completeness target)

A dexterity needs a circling leg and a support leg. Which leg circles is set by the dexterity's side relative to the frame. Two consecutive dexterities therefore make a demand on leg availability: the circling leg of dex2 must be free given the stance dex1 left. The grammar does permit a dexterity to circle with the same foot that set the bag, so same-leg chaining is not universally forbidden; but the grammar does not assert that **every** same-leg or same-side-consecutive pairing leaves a usable stance. Some pairs demand a leg that dex1's stance did not free. The six-cube assumed all 64 pass this test; the grammar warrants neither all-pass nor a specific pruning. **Verdict: prunes, extent grammar-undetermined.** Completeness is not grammar-warranted.

### Axis D — Hidden intermediate state (a completeness-and-identity target)

The grammar tokenizes the middle's side and nothing else. If the real handoff carries state the grammar omits — the bag's height, its rotational phase, the exact stance — then two consequences follow, both fatal to a clean product on a two-valued carriage. First, some pairs are ill-formed because dex1 cannot leave the bag in a middle state dex2 can consume, even when their sides agree (a completeness failure the side-only model cannot see). Second, two distinct six-cube addresses can be the same movement whenever they differ only in a middle component the terminal never records, or two movements the grammar would call distinct collapse to one address (an identity failure). The six-cube is exactly correct only if side is the complete intermediate state, which the grammar asserts by omission and does not prove. **Verdict: prunes or identifies; the product is over a state the grammar has not shown to be complete.**

### Axis E — The far-receiver grade condition (the separability target)

The grammar carries a conditional grade that fires when the receiving foot is far rather than near. In a chain, whether dex2's receiver is far is set by the carriage and stance dex1 produced. So dex2's grade is a function of dex1, not of dex2's cube vertex alone. This is a **cross-term**: a coordinate's difficulty is not the pair of its two vertices' intrinsic difficulties. If the far condition is only a grade bump, separability of scoring already fails. If the far condition also gates whether that dexterity form is well-formed at all, then dex1's output can render a dex2 vertex ill-formed, and completeness fails too. Either way the product is not "unrestricted": at minimum its grading does not factor. **Verdict: entangles (confirmed); prunes (plausible).** Separability is false.

### Axis F — Direction composition (a completeness target that survives)

Two dexterities carry directions independently in the token set, and the grammar has no rotational-momentum token, so within the grammar there is no direction-continuity constraint to impose. This is the one hidden-variable candidate the grammar actively declines to constrain: direction is a free token on each dexterity. **Verdict: survives.** Direction composition is unrestricted.

### Axis G — Output-side relativity (a labeling target)

Whether dex2's output side is read absolutely or relative to its input changes how the terminal side is computed from the two vertices, but not how many vertices there are. If it is relative, the terminal-side readout is a cross-term of the two flips; if absolute, it is dex2's own axis. This does not prune, but under the relative reading it is another place separability of the readout fails. **Verdict: survives in cardinality; contributes to non-separability under one reading.**

### Axis H — The repeated-dex diagonal as a count operator (a homogeneity target)

The grammar carries a count operator. A dexterity performed twice identically can be produced two ways: as two independent dexterity tokens, or as one dexterity under a count of two. These are different grammatical productions, and the eight diagonal vertices of the six-cube (where dex1 equals dex2) are exactly the coordinates that admit the count reading. So the diagonal may not be genuine two-dex product points; it may belong to the one-dex-with-count category. **Verdict: re-categorizes.** The 64 are not a homogeneous product; at least 8 sit on a category boundary.

## 5. Tally

Of the eight axes, two survive, and six damage the claim, each in a distinct way:

| Axis | Target | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| A carriage-side continuity | completeness | survives (conditionally) |
| B frame transport | flatness | twists: product becomes a bundle |
| C stance / same-leg | completeness | prunes (extent undetermined) |
| D hidden intermediate state | completeness / identity | prunes or identifies |
| E far-receiver grade | separability | entangles (confirmed), prunes (plausible) |
| F direction composition | completeness | survives |
| G output relativity | labeling | survives in count |
| H repeated-dex diagonal | homogeneity | re-categorizes |

No single surviving axis rescues "unrestricted Cartesian product," and three independent axes each defeat one of its three hidden commitments: B defeats flatness, E defeats separability, and C or D defeats completeness. H additionally denies homogeneity.

## 6. The revised claim

The strict claim is withdrawn. What the grammar actually supports is:

> Over a fixed surface fiber, the two-dexterity movements are, in their side-direction-output token labeling, **at most** a product of two dexterity cubes, and this holds as a clean unrestricted product only under three assumptions the grammar does not prove: that the reference frame is invariant across the chain (else the space is a twisted cube-bundle, Axis B), that the carriage side is the complete intermediate state (else it prunes or identifies, Axes C and D), and that the far-receiver condition and same-leg availability never gate well-formedness (else it prunes, Axes C and E). Independently of those assumptions, the space's **grading is provably non-separable** (Axis E), and its **diagonal is grammatically ambiguous** (Axis H). The vertex-set cardinality is therefore an **upper bound of 64**, not an exact count, and the object is better described as a scoring-entangled, possibly-twisted cube-bundle with a re-categorizable diagonal than as a six-cube.

Direction composition (Axis F) is the one part that is unconditionally free.

## 7. What this means for the atlas

Volume II is frozen and is not rewritten; this study is its erratum. Three corrections propagate forward:

- **Cardinality is an upper bound.** Volume II's 64-per-fiber and its census totals should be read as upper bounds on distinct well-formed two-dex coordinates, not exact counts. The true count is 64 minus whatever Axes C, D, and E prune and whatever D identifies, and the grammar does not fix that number.
- **The product descriptor is downgraded to a bundle.** "Unrestricted Cartesian product of two cubes" is replaced by "cube-bundle over the first cube," flat only if the frame is invariant. Later single-movement volumes should carry the frame-transport question as an explicit open axis, because it recurs at every added dexterity and compounds.
- **Grade is not a per-vertex property.** The far-receiver entanglement means a two-dex coordinate's difficulty cannot be computed from its two vertices alone; it depends on the handoff. Any later volume that scores multi-dexterity coordinates must treat grade as a property of the whole path, not of its parts.

The lesson generalizes past two dexterities. The six-cube was seductive because the carriage-side thread composed freely, and Volume II mistook the freedom of the one modeled axis for the freedom of the whole handoff. The handoff carries more than the grammar tokenizes, the frame may turn, and the grade couples across the join. Adding a dexterity multiplies the token labeling by a cube, but it does not multiply the movement space by a cube; the join is where the structure hides, and it is not free.
