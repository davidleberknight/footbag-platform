## Part B: proposed glossary section

Proposed for the existing glossary architecture (it fits naturally alongside "Reading the Dictionary" or at the head of "Movement Basics"). Proposal only; do not edit the glossary. The exact proposed copy:

---

### Movement Language

Freestyle is a language of movement. A trick is not one indivisible thing with a name; it is built from a small set of recurring movement components, a way the bag is set, one or more turns of the leg around it, and the surface it comes to rest on, put together in order. Once you can read the components, you can read almost any trick.

Names help players talk to each other, but a name on its own does not settle what a trick is. Two players can call the same movement by different names, and the same name can travel between movements over time. What identifies a trick is its structure, the components it is made of and how they combine, not the label attached to it. That is why this dictionary is organized by structure, and treats names as the words players use to point at those structures.

Because identity lives in the structure, different names can describe the same or an equivalent movement. When they do, the dictionary keeps them as one trick under a single main name, with the others recorded as alternate names. The notation on a trick's page is a compact record of its scored and family-defining structure: what counts toward its difficulty, and what places it among its relatives. It is a shorthand for reading and scoring a trick, not a full account of every physical detail of how a body performs it.

This dictionary documents the movement vocabulary the community has recognized and named. It is a deep record, but it is not a fence around what is possible. Players have always found new combinations, and the language is built to keep describing them as they do.
