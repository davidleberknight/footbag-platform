Prototype notes — candidate-core-family ambiguity

What this prototype is testing

Whether the UI can hold two competing structural readings for the same row without confusing the reader. The dictionary commits to Reading A; the proposal flags the row for Red review of Reading B. The page needs to surface both without picking a winner.

What feels successful

  • The two-column "competing readings" decomposition is the strongest novel pattern in the prototype suite. It makes the structural disagreement explicit and pedagogical.
  • The "cascade" callout connects the core-family decision to its downstream consequences (status changes, lineage changes, policy-token introduction). One decision, three knock-on effects — and the UI surfaces all three.
  • Reading B's note about the cascade going from exact → approximate + policy_dependent is honest about why the editorial decision is hard. It's not "blur is wrong" — it's "blur's structural shape has two valid readings with different downstream implications."
  • The two-view family lineage handles both worlds. Reading A shows blur in place; Reading B's note explains it would be removed.

What feels overloaded

  • The page is the most information-dense of the suite. Two readings, two lineages, four candidate-core siblings cross-listed — the cognitive load is high. A learner-mode disclosure toggle would help: "show me Reading A only" by default, "show me both" for editors.
  • The "Other candidate-core-family rows" cross-list at the bottom is structurally honest but introduces a meta-concept (the candidate set itself) that has no place else on the page. A "this row's candidate-status is part of a 5-row review pool" framing might help; otherwise the cross-list feels orphan.
  • Reading B's SET (QUANTUM) notation block on a row that the dictionary calls a self-atom is jarring — the page essentially shows "what blur WOULD be if ratified differently." This is editorially useful but pedagogically confusing for non-editorial users.

Ontology concerns exposed

  • The candidate-core-family decision affects display, not just storage. Whichever reading wins, the page contents shift substantially. Phase-3 implementation should plan for a reactive re-render when Red rules — not a code-change deployment.
  • 5 candidates × 2 readings = 10 possible page states. Each candidate-core-family in PROPOSAL §13.4 has its own pre/post-Red shape. The prototype demonstrates the pattern for blur; barfly/blender/ripwalk/stepping-* will need similar treatment. Worth pre-rendering all 10 and bundling into a single "Candidate Core-Family Review" packet for Red.
  • Reading B's downstream cascade is the actual editorial cost. If we tell Red "blur could be quantum-mirage," the question isn't just structural — it's "are you OK with the policy_dependent + approximate consequences?" The packet for Red should make this cost explicit, not just the reading.
