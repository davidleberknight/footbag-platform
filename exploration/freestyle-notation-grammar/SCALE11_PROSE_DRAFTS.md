## 1. parkwalk — 4 ADD, butterfly family (pixie + ss)

**Rationale.** Walking-family closure: parkwalk is the 5th -walk pilot after ripwalk + sidewalk + bigwalk + dimwalk. Direct sibling of dimwalk (same modifier: pixie; same base: butterfly) distinguished only by direction (parkwalk = same-side; dimwalk = natural direction). Mirrors the sidewalk:ripwalk pair (both stepping + butterfly; same-side vs natural-direction).

**short_description**
The pixie-modified butterfly pattern executed with same-side direction throughout — both wing arcs travel on one side of the body rather than crossing through neutral.

**execution_summary**
The opening kick lands same-side outside in the butterfly's wing pattern, identical to a natural-direction butterfly start. The pixie compaction tightens the dex moment: the foot's path through the wing is shorter, the bag's arc more enclosed. Critically, the body holds same-side direction across both wing arcs — where dimwalk allows the body to drift through neutral between kicks, parkwalk locks the direction.

The second wing arrives on the same body line as the first. The recovery lands in an opposite clipper cross-body delay with the body still oriented same-side throughout the bag's travel.

**learning_notes**
Same-side discipline is the canonical identity. Drift toward neutral mid-trick and the compound slides toward dimwalk — the dictionary treats direction as a structural distinguisher, not a stylistic preference. A learner who has dimwalk in their vocabulary will feel the natural-direction body urge mid-wing; resisting that urge is the parkwalk-specific challenge.

The pixie compaction must hold across both wing arcs. Releasing the compaction between kicks turns parkwalk into a generic same-side butterfly with a pixie-tagged opening; the second wing must inherit the pixie discipline from the first.

**prerequisite_notes**
Butterfly (3 ADD, SCALE-6 pilot) provides the wing-motion base. Dimwalk (4 ADD, SCALE-4 pilot) is the natural-direction sibling — same modifier, same base, opposite direction commitment. Sidewalk (4 ADD, SCALE-8 pilot) is the same-side analog in the stepping-butterfly cluster; the parkwalk:dimwalk pair runs parallel to sidewalk:ripwalk, with pixie compaction in place of the stepping action. Once dimwalk holds cleanly with natural-direction commitment, parkwalk becomes the inverse-direction discipline on the same modifier line.

## 2. bullwhip — 5 ADD, bullwhip family (self-atom)

**Rationale.** Third pilot in the §3.2 stated-ADD-without-stated-structure policy class (nemesis + jani-walker + bullwhip). Asserted = 5 with no canonical modifier+base decomposition exposed in the public dictionary; canonical reading sits at the row level rather than at the modifier-table-arithmetic level. Vocabulary must differ from nemesis (SCALE-9) and jani-walker (SCALE-10) cadences while honoring the same disposition.

**short_description**
A five-ADD self-contained compound whose canonical reading sits at the row level — bullwhip's mechanics carry the full asserted value without reducing cleanly to a simple modifier-plus-base decomposition.

**execution_summary**
The set initiates with the bullwhip body intensity — a whip-like kick chain that traverses the body from one orientation to another in a single composed sequence. The opening kick carries the initial whip arc; the body continues through the mid-trick rotation while the second and third dex moments land on the inbound trajectory. The recovery delays on the opposite side with the body squared to the new orientation.

The mechanics are committed end-to-end. There is no mid-trick reset, no body neutral between segments — bullwhip's identity is the whip continuity from initial kick to final delay. The five-ADD weight reflects the sustained intensity rather than a modifier-by-modifier additive accounting.

**learning_notes**
Reading bullwhip through the modifier table produces a lower number than the asserted five. The compound belongs to a small dictionary cohort where the row's editorial truth carries weight beyond what the modifier+base composition formula would produce — the kick chain's whip continuity earns the row its asserted value at the row level, not at the modifier-arithmetic level.

Nemesis and jani-walker (both pilot) carry the same disposition pattern. Bullwhip joins them as a teaching cluster for how the dictionary handles compounds whose canonical reading is row-asserted rather than modifier-derived. The three rows are similar in spirit but distinct in mechanics: nemesis runs the furious-rotational line on a barfly double-loop base; jani-walker runs the barraging body intensity on butterfly's wing-motion; bullwhip runs the whip-continuity chain in its own family.

**prerequisite_notes**
No prereq base at pilot; bullwhip is its own family entry. Nemesis (6 ADD, SCALE-9 pilot) and jani-walker (5 ADD, SCALE-10 pilot) are the closest cohort-coherence references — both rows whose asserted value carries through their own canonical reading rather than through the standard modifier+base arithmetic. Once a learner sees the disposition pattern across all three, the row-level-asserted reading becomes a recognizable category of dictionary entry rather than an anomaly.

## 3. barrage — 3 ADD, barrage family (self-atom)

**Rationale.** Standalone trick in its own mechanical family, distinct from the barraging modifier. Description literally lists "Barraging Leg Over = Flurry" and "Barraging Paradox Mirage = Fury" — modifier-action examples that surface the trick-vs-modifier distinction directly. Mirrors terrage's SCALE-10 prose discipline; vary the framing axis to avoid cadence echo.

**short_description**
A standalone three-ADD trick in its own mechanical family — barrage stands apart from the barraging modifier's compound-building role, with the trick form carrying its own canonical execution rather than acting on a base.

**execution_summary**
The set initiates with the barrage body commitment — a specific kick-and-recovery sequence anchored on the trick's own canonical mechanics rather than on a base + modifier composition. The opening kick traces the barrage arc; the body completes a single composed motion that resolves on the opposite clipper delay. The three-ADD weight belongs to the row, not to a build-up across components.

The naming proximity to the barraging modifier is the most common source of confusion. The modifier acts on bases to produce compounds — Barraging Leg Over reads as Flurry (a separate dictionary row), and Barraging Paradox Mirage reads as Fury (another separate row). Barrage as a standalone is none of these compounds; it is its own canonical execution.

**learning_notes**
A learner who knows fury or flurry may approach barrage expecting a barraging-modifier compound on some base. Barrage does not work this way. The trick is complete in its own right — there is no implied base, no modifier action on something else, just barrage's own mechanical line.

The mechanical parallel to other trick-vs-modifier pairs runs deeper than naming. Terrage stands apart from the terraging modifier in the same way; barrage stands apart from barraging. Both pairings teach the same dictionary structure: a trick form and a modifier form can share a vocabulary root yet carry distinct ADD profiles and distinct canonical mechanics.

**prerequisite_notes**
No base prereq at pilot; barrage is its own family entry. Fury (5 ADD, SCALE-7 pilot) and flurry (non-pilot; pending data reconciliation) are the barraging-modifier compounds that the description names as illustrations — those rows show what the barraging modifier produces when applied to bases (paradox+mirage in fury's case; legover in flurry's). Reading barrage as a trick alongside those modifier-compound examples gives the cleanest entry to the distinction.

## 4. rev-whirl — 3 ADD, whirl family (reverse direction)

**Rationale.** Reverse-direction variant of whirl; 2nd canonical direction-variant pair in the dictionary after drifter (SCALE-5) / reverse-drifter (SCALE-10). Records sort_name "Whirl (rev) (ss)" — direction (rev) and side (ss) qualifiers explicit; ss=+0 per dictionary canonical math. Pilot dep of surgery (SCALE-8); rev-whirl is the only non-pilot row currently referenced as `base_trick` by a pilot compound.

**short_description**
Whirl's continuous rotation executed in reverse direction — the same rotational arc as standard whirl, the body's spin reading counter to whirl's natural travel.

**execution_summary**
The opening kick initiates whirl's rotational pattern but with the body's spin oriented counter-direction to standard whirl. The bag's path follows the same rotational arc; the body's rotation carries opposite. The kick mechanics, the contact surfaces, and the recovery position are conserved end-to-end — only the body's rotational direction differs.

The recovery lands with the body oriented opposite to a standard whirl's recovery position. Like reverse-drifter, the trick's identity carries through the directional commitment: the kick alone does not differentiate rev-whirl from whirl; only the body's rotational sense does.

**learning_notes**
Direction is a canonical-row determiner across the dictionary's rotational family. A whirl executed with the body rotating the wrong way is rev-whirl, not a sloppy whirl — and vice versa. The two rows share their kick shape and their three-ADD weight; only the body's rotational sense defines which row a given execution belongs to.

Reverse-drifter (SCALE-10 pilot) carries the same directional-canonical pattern on a different base. The drifter:reverse-drifter pair and the whirl:rev-whirl pair together establish the direction-is-structural rule in practice — once a learner can hold direction commitment unambiguously across two base families, the rule generalizes to other rotational pairs that may surface in future dictionary expansion.

**prerequisite_notes**
Whirl (3 ADD, foundation pilot) is the direct sibling — same kick shape, same ADD weight, opposite rotational direction. Reverse-drifter (3 ADD, SCALE-10 pilot) is the cross-family direction-variant analog — different base (drifter's miraging-clipper line versus whirl's rotational arc) but same directional-canonical rule. Surgery (6 ADD, SCALE-8 pilot) uses rev-whirl as its base; the surgery prereq landing closes once rev-whirl holds at pilot.

## 5. double-around-the-world — 3 ADD, atw family (multiplicity exception)

**Rationale.** Pt8 community-stabilized multiplicity exception. Joins double-leg-over (SCALE-9 pilot) as the second of three "double X" canonical exceptions at pilot tier; third (double-spin, 2 ADD) remains non-pilot. Records sort_name "ATW (in) (double)" — multiplicity explicit.

**short_description**
Two consecutive full leg circles from a single set — a community-stabilized doubling of the around-the-world pattern, recorded as its own canonical row rather than collapsing into a "double X" descriptor.

**execution_summary**
The set arrives with enough height to carry two full leg circles. The first leg circles the bag in the around-the-world pattern; before the bag drops, the kicking leg initiates the second circle on the same trajectory. The body holds posture through both passes — the second circle inherits the rotational momentum of the first without a mid-trick reset.

The recovery lands on the opposite-side delay with both circles complete. Same-side execution distinguishes the canonical double-around-the-world from a cross-body double pattern; the leg's path stays on one side of the body throughout the sequence.

**learning_notes**
Set height is the load-bearing variable. A standard around-the-world height leaves no time for the second circle; the canonical execution requires a set that gives the leg two full-circle windows. Set too low, and the second circle arrives after the bag drops; set too high, and the rotational timing decouples from the bag's path.

The doubling convention is what earns the canonical row. A double around-the-world run informally (as if it were just "around-the-world ×2") collapses into an undifferentiated sequence; the canonical row treatment expects the doubling to be deliberate — two consecutive full circles composed as one sustained pattern, not two separate trick attempts.

**prerequisite_notes**
Around-the-world (2 ADD, foundation pilot) is the single-circle base. The multiplicity discipline (running the same trick twice off one set without resetting body position) is the canonical analog of double-leg-over (3 ADD, SCALE-9 pilot) — dictionary rows where a community-stabilized doubling convention earns its own named row rather than collapsing into a "double X" descriptor. Double-spin (2 ADD, non-pilot) is the third member of the multiplicity-exception cohort; the three rows together establish how the dictionary handles stabilized doubling.

## 6. high-plains-drifter — 4 ADD, clipper-stall family (self-atom)

**Rationale.** Standalone 4-ADD compound on the clipper-stall base. Description: "Two-dex compound from clipper stall." No aliases, no modifier-table dependencies. Clean self-atom with clipper-stall pilot foundation.

**short_description**
Two consecutive dexes worked from a clipper-stall plant — a four-ADD compound whose execution sits between the clipper stall's controlled start and a same-side recovery.

**execution_summary**
The clipper stall holds the bag in a controlled cross-body position before the trick begins. From that plant, the kicking foot delivers two consecutive dex flicks: the first redirects the bag along its initial trajectory, the second extends the redirection while the body line completes its travel. The two dex moments arrive in close sequence rather than separated by a body action or a reset.

The recovery lands on the same-side delay with the body re-centered. The four-ADD weight comes from the clipper-stall plant plus the two-dex sequence plus the same-side recovery; the rhythm is sequential rather than gap-separated.

**learning_notes**
The two-dex sequence is the load-bearing element. Treating the dexes as one continuous flick collapses the trick into a single-dex compound on clipper-stall — a different row entirely. The two dex moments must read as distinct kicks separated by enough rhythmic space to register as a sequence.

Pacing matters more than power. The first dex sets the bag's redirection; the second dex extends the redirection with the body line already in motion. Rushing the second dex blends the two; over-spacing them creates an audible pause that disrupts the trick's continuity. The execution sits between those failure modes — distinct but tight.

**prerequisite_notes**
Clipper-stall (2 ADD, SCALE-6 pilot) provides the controlled plant. The two-dex rhythm is the row's defining characteristic; learners who can chain two dex flicks from any controlled-plant primitive (toe stall, clipper stall, sole stall) have the kinematic vocabulary for high-plains-drifter once they apply that rhythm specifically off the clipper-stall plant. No direct modifier-bridge sibling at pilot; the row stands alone on its base.
