# Doctrine Paper 4 — The Frontier

## Purpose

Define the boundaries of freestyle: the ceiling of difficulty, the completeness
of the grammar, the treatment of structures that are valid but unnamed, and what
remains for future players to invent. This is where the theory ends and the open
territory begins. It is also where two questions the other papers deferred come
to rest: whether a 9-ADD trick is possible, and whether every legal structure is
a real trick.

## Settled premises

- **The dictionary records vocabulary, not combinatorics.** A trick is admitted
  when it is attested in a tracked source, not because it can be composed. The
  operator grammar can generate thousands of legal compounds; the encyclopedia
  does not canonize a compound merely because the grammar permits it. This is the
  source-attestation rule, and it is what keeps the canon finite and real.
- **Frequency is evidence, never authority.** Recurrence in a corpus documents
  that a name is used; it does not by itself promote the name to canon.
- **The current ADD ceiling is 8.** Three canonical rows reach it. Nothing above
  is cleanly attested. Every published 9 reading was investigated, and each fails
  one of two ways: it rests on an X-Dex application the settled rule forbids, or on
  an operator the grammar has not defined. The doctrine already decides them (see
  the worked examples). "Not yet built" is a status fact, never a gap.

## Open questions

1. **Is there a practical upper limit to ADD, and is a 9 physically possible?**
   The record documents nothing clean above 8. Whether that is a limit of
   documentation or of the body is not an editorial question — it needs people
   who train and judge at the ceiling, and any answer is dated, because athletes
   change what is possible. (Elite players and competition judges first; Red for
   how such a trick would be read if it appeared.)
2. **Is the operator grammar complete, and does it permit repetition?** Whether
   the primitive operator set is closed, and whether an operator may repeat in
   one compound (miraging twice), is likely answerable only as a dated editorial
   closure rather than a discovery: the grammar is complete as of a stated date,
   until a new operator is attested. Framing it as discoverable assumes a fact
   the record cannot supply. (Red for whether the set is closed; internal
   editorial for the dated-closure framing.)
3. **How should a structure that is valid but unnamed be treated?** The grammar
   permits, and players can perform, structures no community has named (the
   "atomic kick" class). Are they real tricks the encyclopedia should document,
   observational entries, or nothing until named? The attestation premise leans
   toward "nothing until attested," but the boundary — an obvious structure with
   no name yet — is the genuine question. (Internal editorial doctrine, since it
   is the encyclopedia's own admission policy; Red only where naming a structure
   would itself be a movement ruling.)
4. **What should future generations still be able to invent?** A deliberately
   open question: the encyclopedia should not close territory that the sport has
   not yet explored. This is a value the frontier holds, not a rule to settle.

## Worked examples

- **Big Apple Sauce, the X-Dex 9.** Spinning Paradox Miraging Symposium
  Torque is scored 9 by FootbagMoves and independently notated at 9, on the
  strength of an `[XDEX]` bracket. The platform publishes 8, because under the
  settled X-Dex rule that bonus cannot fire here: no qualifying set is present,
  and the receiving dex follows a paradox dex. This 9 is
  therefore not a discovery the encyclopedia is missing; it is a divergence the
  scoring rule already resolves. This is the whole ADD-ceiling question in one
  case: publishing a first 9 would mean reversing a settled rule, not filling a
  gap.
- **Carousel, the undefined-operator 9.** Surging Ducking Paradox Symposium
  Whirling Rake is likewise listed at 9, but its ninth point rests on the
  `surging` operator, which the grammar has not defined. The platform publishes 8
  and holds the ninth point pending a structural definition of surging — not a
  settled rule this time, but the attestation premise: an undefined operator is
  not a scored one.
- **Thigh Catch, the unnamed-structure case.** A between-the-thighs catch is
  mechanically admissible — unusual-surface stalls are canonical vocabulary — but
  no tracked source attests it under any name. Under the attestation premise it
  is not yet trackable, however obvious the structure. It is the clean test of
  question 3: valid, performable, unattested.
- **Grammar repetition.** Only a few corpus names repeat an operator, and most
  reduce to a single trick. The thinness of the evidence is why completeness is a
  declaration, not a discovery.

## Encyclopedia impact

The frontier governs the shape of the Emerging Vocabulary surface and the History
page's closing argument. A physical-ceiling answer dates gracefully and frames how
the high-ADD tail is presented. A grammar-completeness declaration lets the
encyclopedia state, with a date, that its operator set is closed — turning an open
worry into a bounded claim. The unnamed-structure policy decides what the
observational tier admits.

## Who should review it

The physical ceiling belongs at least as much to active elite players and
competition judges as to Red, and it should be dated. Grammar completeness and
repetition are Red's, framed as a dated closure. The unnamed-structure admission
policy is internal editorial doctrine. What-remains-inventable is a value the
maintainer holds open, not a question to answer.
