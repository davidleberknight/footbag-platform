# Doctrine Paper 0 — Charter, Scope, and Amendment

## Purpose

This is the front matter for the freestyle doctrine series. It states what the
series covers, the premises every later paper is allowed to assume, who reviews
what, and the rule by which a future answer amends a past one. The papers age;
this charter is the part that has to endure, because it governs how the rest is
changed.

The series is deliberately small. Five doctrine papers and this charter are the
minimum needed to define singles freestyle vocabulary for the next twenty years:
Identity, Notation, Scoring, Frontier, and History. Each has one job. When a
question could sit in two papers, this charter's separations decide.

## Scope

In scope: the vocabulary of individual freestyle tricks — how they are told
apart (Identity), written down (Notation), scored (Scoring), bounded (Frontier),
and how the vocabulary came to be (History).

Reserved, by decision rather than omission:

- **Run and sequence vocabulary** — density, the guiltless / tiltless / godly
  family, streaks, connectors. A run is composed of tricks; its own vocabulary
  is a later body of work and is not treated here.
- **Doubles and the shred circle.** Partner and group play have their own
  conventions.
- **Kick-game, net, and golf vocabulary.** Separate disciplines.
- **Competition scoring rules.** ADD is a structural reading of a trick; how a
  routine is judged in competition is owned by the IFPA rulebook, not by this
  series. Scoring (Paper 3) defines what ADD measures, not how a judge scores a
  bag.

A question that turns out to belong to a reserved area is noted and set aside,
not answered inside a trick-vocabulary paper.

## Two genres: doctrine and testimony

The series contains two kinds of statement, and they must never be read as the
same kind.

- **Doctrine** — rulings and rules in force. Papers 1 through 4 are doctrine.
  A doctrine statement prescribes.
- **Testimony** — the historical account of how the vocabulary actually grew.
  Paper 5 is testimony. A testimony statement explains; it never prescribes.
  "This is how operators proliferated" is not "this is how operators must be
  admitted."

A first edition that blurs the two invites a future reader to cite a description
of what happened as a rule for what must happen. Paper 5 carries this warning in
its own front matter.

## Settled premises (assumed by every later paper)

These are in force today, implemented in the encyclopedia, and are stated here
once as premises. Later papers build on them and do not re-open them. Each names
the paper that owns its detail.

1. **A trick has one true name and may have descriptive sub-names.** The
   canonical name is singular; structural and notation readings are secondary
   and searchable; historical variants are aliases, never co-equal canonicals.
   (Identity.)
2. **Map, do not delete.** A name genuinely used is preserved and mapped, never
   discarded for obscurity. (Identity, History.)
3. **Direction is structural.** A direction-reversed movement is its own trick,
   not a qualifier: mirage is not illusion, around-the-world is not orbit.
   (Identity.)
4. **Side configuration is structural.** A positional name's identity is its
   resolved side configuration, not the qualifier-stripped name. (Identity,
   Notation.)
5. **Every canonical trick terminates in one of the twelve core atoms.** A
   modifier-only sequence is not a trick. (Notation.)
6. **Sets versus operators is a question of role.** A concept is a set if it
   launches a trick and an operator if it modifies one, regardless of the
   mechanic it embeds. (Identity, Scoring.)
7. **The bracket count of the operational notation equals the published ADD.**
   Enforced on every authored row. (Notation, Scoring.)
8. **The ADD-authority order.** The published value is official; the additive
   arithmetic is diagnostic; an outside source's number is recorded, never
   adopted. (Scoring.)
9. **Two operator merges are settled precedent, not open questions:** Atomic =
   Illusioning (the same outward-dex operator; the Illusioning name is a
   descriptive reading and its registry row is retired) and Furious = Barraging
   (the same two-dex uptime set; Barraging is the more explanative name). They
   are the worked precedents the general merger criterion must retro-justify.
   (Identity.)

## Source reconciliation (editorial epistemology, not a scoring rule)

The encyclopedia draws on several outside lineages — FootbagMoves, footbag.org,
PassBack, Stanford — that disagree with each other and with the platform. How
those disagreements are handled is editorial epistemology, governed here rather
than inside any one paper, because Identity uses it to weigh merger evidence,
Scoring uses it for ADD divergence, and Frontier uses it for the lone 9 signal.
It is deliberately kept out of Scoring: which number wins is a scoring rule; how
a divergence is treated as evidence is not.

- **Record, do not adopt.** Where an outside source diverges, the platform
  publishes its own structural value and records the divergence in provenance,
  in the operator-weight divergence policy, or in the per-trick divergence
  registry. The divergence is documented and explained, never silently absorbed
  or silently overridden.
- **Frequency is evidence, never authority.** Recurrence documents use; it does
  not confer canon (repeated in Frontier, where it also bounds the corpus).
- **A single source is non-authoritative for a contested value.** When the
  platform, footbag.org, and one other source agree and a fourth diverges, the
  outlier is observational, not decisive.
- **Weight corroboration by independent lineage, not source count.** Two sources
  that re-notated one shared list are one line of evidence, not two.

This is internal editorial doctrine. It does not need the rules expert; it needs
an author to apply it consistently, which the divergence surfaces already do.

## Amendment rule

The series changes by supersession, never by silent overwrite.

1. When an answer lands, integrate it into the owning paper, record it in
   `RED_RULINGS.md` (or the relevant operational doctrine document), and remove
   the question from `RED_QUEUE.md`. No tombstones.
2. The superseded reading is named as superseded, with what replaced it, so the
   chain is amendable. A reading that once governed and no longer does is marked,
   not erased.
3. **A ruling that merely ratifies text drafted for it is not primary grounding.**
   New doctrine traces to the sources, the expert, or an explicit human decision,
   never to a paper quoting itself back.
4. A settled premise is re-opened only deliberately and on the record, never as a
   side effect of answering something else. The two merge precedents (premise 9)
   and the authority order (premise 8) are the ones most at risk of accidental
   re-litigation; a paper that appears to re-open them is a drafting error to fix.

## Open questions

- **Which body of vocabulary is formalized next.** Runs and sequence vocabulary
  is the largest reserved area and the most likely second edition. This is a
  scope decision for the maintainer, not a doctrine question.

## Worked examples

- **The atomic ADD model.** The old "atomic is +2 on rotational bases" reading
  governed for a time; it was superseded by "atomic is +1 flat, with a separate
  conditional X-Dex," and the change was recorded, the registry migrated, and the
  old reading marked superseded. This is the amendment rule working as intended.
- **The Illusioning row.** When Atomic absorbed Illusioning, the Illusioning
  registry row was retired to zero and the name kept only as a descriptive
  reading. A merge executed cleanly, with the losing name preserved as an alias.

## Encyclopedia impact

This charter is the constitution of the doctrine series. `AUTHORITY.md` (which
names where each datum lives) and the operational documents (`RED_RULINGS.md`,
`RED_QUEUE.md`, the family and positional rulings) are downstream of it. Nothing
in the encyclopedia should contradict a settled premise here without a recorded
amendment.

## Who should review it

Internal editorial (the maintainer). The reserved-scope boundaries that touch
competition — the line between ADD and judged scoring — are worth an IFPA
confirmation, but the charter itself is an editorial instrument.
