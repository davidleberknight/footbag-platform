# The 2-ADD Universe as a Five-Dimensional Surface Lattice: Which Fibers Preserve the Cube

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. This note reframes the 2-ADD dexterity cube as a five-dimensional lattice with two surface axes, and asks which launch-and-landing surface fibers preserve the full eight-vertex cube and which break it. Companion file: `two_add_surface_fibers.csv`. Grounded in the operational notation of the live 1-ADD and 2-ADD movements.

Working axioms carried forward: a trick is a movement formula, names are annotations, the kick is worth one ADD less than its stall counterpart, and the dictionary is one observed subset of a larger universe.

---

## 1. The five-dimensional lattice

The prior slice showed that the dominant 2-ADD schema, one dexterity followed by one plain landing, is a cube in three binary coordinates: dex side (SAME or OP), dex direction (IN or OUT), and terminal side (SAME or OP). That cube was studied at a single fixed pair of surfaces (launch from the toe, land on the toe).

Restore the two surface coordinates and the schema is a five-dimensional lattice:

```
launch_surface  x  dex_side  x  dex_direction  x  terminal_side  x  landing_surface
       S               2              2                 2                 S
```

where `S` is the surface set. The three binary axes in the middle are the cube. The two surface axes at the ends select a **fiber**: fix a launch surface and a landing surface, and what remains is a cube (or a degenerate remnant of one). The question of this note is a question about fibers: over which (launch, landing) pairs does the full eight-vertex cube survive as physically realizable, bag-controlled, grade-2 movement, and over which does it collapse, lift, or partially foreclose.

The answer is governed by three independent properties of the two surfaces, so the analysis is not fourteen-by-fourteen case work; it is three rules.

---

## 2. Three properties of a surface that decide a fiber

Reading the operational notation of every stall and every kick in the live data separates the surfaces along three axes that turn out to be exactly the three that control the cube.

**Grade of the landing.** A plain landing writes as `SURFACE [DEL]` and scores 1. A hard landing writes with an extra bracket and scores 2: cross-body `[XBD][DEL]` (the clipper), or unusual-surface `[UNS][DEL]` (cloud, heel, sole). Plain landings in the data: toe, inside, outside, knee, neck, head, forehead, shoulder, peak. Hard landings: clipper, cloud, heel, sole.

**Laterality of the landing.** A paired surface exists on both legs or both sides, so the bag can be caught on the SAME side as the plant foot or the OPposite side; the terminal-side coordinate has two values. A midline surface is a single central target, so terminal side has one value. Paired: toe, inside, outside, knee, sole, heel, shoulder, clipper. Midline: head, forehead, neck, cloud, peak.

**Symmetry of the launch.** A central launch (the toe set) commits no side, so both dex-side values and both directions are open. A self-lateral launch (setting and landing on the same paired surface) is likewise symmetric. A cross-body launch (the clipper set) commits the body to an opposite-side, across-the-body configuration before the dexterity begins, which forecloses part of the cube if bag control is to be kept.

These three properties act on three different parts of the cube, which is why they compose cleanly.

---

## 3. The preservation rule

A fiber (launch, landing) carries the full eight-vertex 2-ADD cube if and only if all three hold:

1. **The landing is plain** (grade 1). Otherwise the dexterity plus the landing is grade 3, and the cube still exists but has moved one grade up; there is no eight-vertex cube at 2 ADD on this fiber.
2. **The landing is paired** (lateral). Otherwise the terminal-side axis has a single value and the cube halves from eight vertices to a four-vertex square in dex-side by direction.
3. **The launch is symmetric** (central or self-lateral). Otherwise a cross-body launch forecloses one dex-side face of the cube, leaving a partial four-vertex remnant.

Each failing condition removes a different axis, so the failures stack predictably. This is the whole result; the rest is reading it off the surfaces.

---

## 4. The three phases of the landing dimension

Sliding the landing surface while holding a symmetric launch sorts every surface into one of three phases.

**Phase I, cube-preserving (plain and paired): toe, inside, outside, knee, shoulder.** The full cube lives here at grade 2. The toe fiber is completely filled and named; the other Phase I fibers are structurally identical cubes that the tradition has barely labeled. Inside-to-inside names one vertex (inside around the world), outside-to-outside names one (outside around the world), and toe-to-inside names two (guay and the inspinning reverse guay). Every other vertex on every Phase I fiber is an open, unnamed coordinate of the universe, not a missing trick but an unlabeled one.

**Phase II, cube-halving (plain and midline): head, forehead, neck, peak.** The dexterity can still reach the surface at grade 2, but there is only one central target, so terminal side is meaningless and the cube is a four-vertex square in dex-side by direction. Nothing is named here; the head and neck stalls in the data are bare landings, not the receiver end of a named dexterity, which is consistent with the square being real but unexplored.

**Phase III, cube-lifting (hard: clipper, cloud, heel, sole).** The landing already scores 2 on its own, so a preceding dexterity pushes the movement to grade 3. There is no 2-ADD cube on these fibers; the cube exists one grade up. This is the structural reason the clipper, which the dictionary treats as a peer of the toe, does not host the eight foundational atoms: as a landing it is a cross-body grade-2 event, so its dexterity cube is a grade-3 object, not a grade-2 one. Among Phase III surfaces the paired ones (heel, sole, clipper) keep their terminal-side axis at grade 3, while the midline one (cloud) both lifts and halves.

---

## 5. The launch dimension

Holding a Phase I landing and sliding the launch:

**Symmetric launches (toe, and self-lateral inside or outside): full cube.** The canonical toe-to-toe fiber is the reference. Inside-to-inside and outside-to-outside are the same cube on paired plain self-fibers.

**Cross-body launch (clipper): partial cube.** The clipper set commits an opposite-side, across-the-body position, so the SAME dex-side face of the cube cannot be entered while keeping the bag under control; only the OP dex-side face survives. The single named clipper-launch member in the data, bubba, sits on that surviving face (opposite out, opposite terminal), which is exactly where the rule predicts the culture would find a namable trick. The clipper launch therefore does not preserve the cube; it halves it along dex side, the launch-side analogue of what a midline landing does on the terminal side.

**Cross-surface transfer between contralateral paired surfaces (for example outside to inside): partial cube.** A transfer that crosses from one side of the body to the other is realizable in one handedness but not its mirror, so the side-reflection edge of the cube breaks even though both surfaces are Phase I. Same-surface self-fibers and central-launch transfers do not have this problem because they are symmetric under the reflection.

---

## 6. Reading the fiber matrix

The companion `two_add_surface_fibers.csv` lists representative fibers with their three surface properties, the resulting cube status (preserved, halved, lifted, or partial), the surviving cube dimension, and the count of named vertices. The pattern across it is:

- **Preserved (dimension 3):** toe-to-toe (8 named), toe-to-inside (2), inside-to-inside (1), outside-to-outside (1), and the structurally-open plain-paired fibers toe-to-outside, knee-to-knee, toe-to-knee, toe-to-shoulder (0 named). These are the true home of the 2-ADD cube, and all but the toe fiber are almost entirely unlabeled.
- **Halved by a midline landing (dimension 2):** toe-to-head, toe-to-neck, toe-to-forehead, toe-to-peak. Terminal side is dead.
- **Lifted by a hard landing (cube at grade 3):** toe-to-clipper, toe-to-heel, toe-to-sole, and toe-to-cloud (which also halves).
- **Partial by an asymmetric launch (dimension 2):** clipper-to-toe (1 named, bubba, on the surviving OP face), clipper-to-inside, and contralateral transfers such as outside-to-inside.

---

## 7. What this says about the universe and the observed subset

Three conclusions follow.

First, the eight-vertex cube is not special to the toe; it is special to the class of plain paired surfaces under a symmetric launch. The toe fiber is simply the one the tradition explored to completion. Every other Phase I fiber (inside, outside, knee, shoulder, and the transfers among them) carries the identical cube, and the named tricks occupy a handful of vertices while the rest stand open. The gap between the dictionary and the universe is, in large part, the unfilled Phase I fibers.

Second, the clipper is structurally not a peer of the toe, despite being named alongside it. As a launch it halves the cube along dex side; as a landing it lifts the cube out of grade 2 entirely. Its role in the algebra is the cross-body grade-raiser, not a symmetric surface. This is a concrete, testable structural claim, and it explains why the clipper anchors different families than the toe.

Third, the surface axes are not free parameters that merely relabel a fixed cube. They interact with the cube's internal symmetry: laterality controls the terminal-side reflection, launch handedness controls the dex-side reflection, and grade controls whether the cube sits at 2 ADD at all. The five-dimensional lattice is therefore not a product of a cube and a flat surface plane; it is a cube whose dimensionality is a function of where on the surface plane you stand.

## 8. Open questions for the next slice

- **Confirm the Phase I self-fibers by realizability, not just by rule.** The rule predicts a full cube on inside-to-inside, knee-to-knee, and shoulder-to-shoulder. A physical or expert check of the currently-unnamed vertices would either confirm the prediction or expose a surface-specific constraint the three properties miss.
- **Contralateral transfer symmetry.** The claim that a contralateral transfer breaks the side-reflection edge should be tested against any named transfer trick to see whether both handednesses ever coexist.
- **Peak as a surface.** Peak is treated here as a midline apex; whether it is a genuine landing surface or a timing marker that should sit outside the surface axis is unresolved.
- **The grade-3 cubes.** The Phase III lift means clipper, heel, sole, and cloud host their dexterity cubes at grade 3. Enumerating those is the natural continuation, and it connects to the still-open rotation-grade question from the algebra paper, since the grade-3 space is where whirl and swirl live.
