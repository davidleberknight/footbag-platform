# Hierarchy

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. It determines whether the eight structural families organize into larger superfamilies, and if so how. Companion to `VOLUME_IV.md` and `STRUCTURAL_FAMILY_PROFILES.md`.

## The question

Do the discovered families nest into a hierarchy, the way biological families nest into orders and classes? The answer from the atlas is specific: **they form a lattice, not a tree.** There are superfamilies, but they are not the levels of a nested classification; they are the individual facet axes, and the families are their intersection.

## Why a tree does not fit

A tree of families would require a privileged first split, then a privileged split within each branch, and so on: an ordering of the facets. The uniform-hypercube result forbids exactly that. Because every facet partitions the space evenly and no facet is more natural than another, there is no principled first cut. Splitting first by leg usage, first by receiver behavior, or first by directional relation all give equally valid and equally balanced trees, and none is the tree. When every ordering of the splits is equally good, the structure is not a tree; it is the product of the splits, which is a lattice.

## The lattice

The three defining facets are three independent binary axes, so the eight primary families are the eight vertices of a cube:

```
                 receiver: preserved -------- changed
   leg: same-leg  { F1 F3 }                  { F2 F4 }
   leg: alt-leg   { F5 F7 }                  { F6 F8 }
   direction:       parallel  reversed  (the third axis, into the page)
```

Each family sits at one vertex, defined by its value on all three axes. Each family has exactly three neighbors, reached by flipping one facet. The superfamilies are the faces and edges of this cube:

- **Single-axis superfamilies (the three coarsest cuts).** By leg usage: the same-leg superfamily (F1 to F4) and the alternating-leg superfamily (F5 to F8). By receiver: the preserved superfamily (F1, F3, F5, F7) and the changed superfamily (F2, F4, F6, F8). By direction: the parallel superfamily (F1, F2, F5, F6) and the reversed superfamily (F3, F4, F7, F8). These six half-spaces are the real superfamilies; each is a face of the cube.
- **Two-axis groupings.** Any pair of facets fixed gives a four-family edge grouping (for example same-leg-and-preserved = F1, F3). These are intermediate.
- **The eight primary families.** All three facets fixed: the cube's vertices.

## Finer and coarser than the eight

- **Finer (latent):** the carriage facet (steady versus mid-cross) subdivides each of the eight into two, giving sixteen cells. It is latent because it is the notation-invisible axis (Volume III), so it refines the structure without being observable in the notation.
- **Finer (a special locus):** the repeated-dexterity diagonal is a sub-locus inside the same-leg parallel families (F1 and F2), not a family of its own; it is where dex1 equals dex2.
- **Coarser (the higher layer):** the operator features (co-articulation, body-mediation, paradox, rotation) are additional independent axes, not coarsenings of these facets. Adding them does not collapse the eight families; it multiplies the lattice by more axes.

## The shape of the hierarchy

The two-dex universe does not have a hierarchy in the nested-tree sense, and the atlas says why: its facets are independent, so its classification is a Boolean lattice (a product of binary choices), whose superfamilies are the single-facet half-spaces and whose finest cells are the full-facet intersections. A tree would impose an order on independent axes that the geometry refuses to privilege. The correct picture is a cube of eight families with three orthogonal superfamily axes, refined latently by carriage and multiplied by the higher operator axes, and it is a lattice through and through.
