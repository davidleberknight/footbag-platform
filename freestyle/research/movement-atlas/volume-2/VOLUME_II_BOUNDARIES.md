# Volume II Known Boundaries and Unresolved Questions

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. This file states where Atlas Volume II intentionally stops and which questions it surfaced but did not answer. Naming a boundary is not a plan to cross it. Volume II is complete within its stated bound and silent beyond it.

Volume II maps the two-dexterity movement universe generated by the frozen Grammar v1, over the frozen surface lattice, in its sequential reading. Its edges are of three kinds: edges of scope (the atlas could extend here on the same grammar), edges of knowledge (the grammar has not settled what lies here), and the hard edge (physical realizability, which the atlas never judges).

## Edges of scope

- **Three-and-higher dexterity.** Volume II stops at two dexterities. The fiber law it established (n dexterities give the product of n dexterity cubes, the 3n-cube) predicts the next volumes' geometry, but this volume does not generate them. Each added dexterity multiplies the fiber by another cube and adds another threaded carriage axis.

- **The parameter and body overlay.** Volume II maps pure dexterity structure only. Rotation, paradox, posture, body operators, and the far-receiver condition are the overlay layer (the planned Volume III); they stack on any two-dex coordinate without adding a dexterity, and this volume does not enumerate them. Most named two-dexterity tricks carry such an overlay, which is why this pure-dexterity volume is deliberately name-sparse.

- **Composition between movements.** Volume II maps single movements, each a launch, two dexterities, and a landing. Whether one whole movement can follow another is composition (the planned Volume IV), a different layer that draws edges between atlas locations. The internal handoff studied here (the thread between two dexterities of one move) is not the same as an external transition between two moves, and the boundary between them is the one-move-versus-two-moves question below.

- **The naming cross-walk.** Volume II leaves every coordinate unnamed on purpose. Attaching the sport's pure-two-dexterity names to specific six-cube vertices is a later naming pass or an erratum, because it needs intermediate-carriage data the frozen notation does not always record. That cross-walk references this map; it does not change its geometry.

## Edges of knowledge

- **The simultaneity operator.** Every sequential two-dex coordinate has a simultaneous reading, the two dexterities performed together rather than in order (the layered-step operator). Its scoring rule is unresolved in the frozen grammar, so Volume II maps only the sequential reading as terrain and marks the simultaneous reading as an uncertain overlay across all valid coordinates. Ruling the simultaneity operator would roughly double the mapped terrain or fold it, and that ruling is doctrine work upstream of a future volume, not atlas work.

- **The one-move-versus-two-moves boundary.** A two-dexterity movement is itself a tiny sequence, so there is a real definitional question of where a single move ends and a composition begins. Volume II adopts the frozen grammar's convention (a dexterity chain with no intermediate contact is one move) and does not attempt to settle the general boundary, which composition (Volume IV) must fix before it can distinguish a within-move handoff from a between-move transition.

- **Parameter grades.** As in Volume I, the grades of a rotational dexterity, a paradox, and a no-plant posture are unpinned in the frozen grammar. Volume II maps the plain, planted, non-rotational, non-paradox two-dex core and does not enumerate the parameter variants, because it cannot score them without inventing a rule. They are overlay terrain the map cannot yet grade.

- **The primitive floor.** The pogo and bare-surface-kick questions from the closure work remain open. They do not arise in a pure two-dexterity movement, so Volume II does not touch them, but they still gate any volume that would generate the full parameterized or higher-dexterity space.

## The hard edge

- **Physical realizability.** Volume II never claims a coordinate is or is not physically possible. Grammar-valid means well-formed under the movement language, nothing more. A six-cube vertex is a location in the movement language; whether a human can carry a bag through that intermediate and land it as specified is a biomechanics question (the planned Volume V) that attaches a judgment to each location and must never be read into a `valid` or `unnamed` status here. The dex-swap raises this edge sharply: whether the two orderings of a dexterity pair are the same movement is exactly such a realizability question, and the atlas marks the swap relation without answering it.

## Summary of the edges

| Boundary | Kind | What lies beyond |
|---|---|---|
| Three-and-higher dexterity | scope | the 3n-cube fiber, one more cube per dexterity |
| Parameter and body overlay | scope | rotation, paradox, posture, body operators, far-receiver, stacked on any coordinate |
| Composition between movements | scope | edges between whole movements |
| Naming cross-walk | scope | attaching pure-two-dex names to specific vertices |
| Simultaneity operator | knowledge | the scoring of two dexterities performed together |
| One-move-versus-two-moves | knowledge | where a single move ends and a composition begins |
| Parameter grades | knowledge | how to score rotation, paradox, posture variants |
| Primitive floor (pogo, bare kick) | knowledge | whether a fifth primitive exists |
| Physical realizability | hard edge | whether a body can perform a location, including whether a swap-pair commutes |

Every future branch that touches two-dexterity movement begins at one of these edges, and each can now begin by naming the Volume II locations it concerns rather than reasoning from isolated trick names.
