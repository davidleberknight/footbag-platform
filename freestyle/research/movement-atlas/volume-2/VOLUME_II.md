# The Atlas of the Freestyle Movement Universe, Volume II: the Two-Dex Movement Universe

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. No production code, no dictionary rows, no authoring, no names, no parser or generator redesign, no grammar change. Volume I is frozen and untouched; this volume extends the frozen Grammar v1 and the Volume I coordinate system to exactly two dexterities.

Generated by `generate_volume_2.py`; the machine-readable census is `volume_2_catalog.csv`. Companions: `VOLUME_II_GUIDE.md` (how to read a coordinate), `VOLUME_II_ANALYSIS.md` (the landscape), `VOLUME_II_BOUNDARIES.md` (where the volume stops).

Volume I asked what the topology of one-dexterity movement is, and answered: a surface-fiber bundle whose fiber is the eight-vertex dex cube. Volume II asks the same question of two-dexterity movement. It does not assume the cube generalizes; it lets the geometry emerge and reports what it found.

## 1. The question, and the method

A one-dexterity movement is `launch -> dex -> terminal`, and the dexterity is one cube vertex. A two-dexterity movement is `launch -> dex1 -> dex2 -> terminal`, and the new fact is what sits between the two dexterities: **nothing lands there**. The bag is carried from the first dexterity to the second in an intermediate state, with no contact, no surface, no stall. So the central structural question of this volume is: what is that intermediate, and does it make the two dexterities independent or coupled?

The method, per the atlas discipline, is geometry before generation. Sections 2 through 5 determine the coordinate system, the symmetries, and the constraints from the structure of the movement itself. Only then (Section 6) is the implied space generated and counted.

## 2. The coordinate system

The intermediate between the two dexterities is a **carriage state**: which side the bag is being carried on, relative to the reference foot, the same binary the Volume I cube used for its output side. The bag launches in a carriage state, the first dexterity leaves it in another, and the second dexterity acts on that and leaves it in the terminal state.

This makes a two-dexterity movement an **ordered pair of cube vertices, threaded by carriage state**:

- The first dexterity is a cube vertex `(side1, direction1, out1)`. Its output side `out1` is the intermediate carriage.
- The second dexterity is a cube vertex `(side2, direction2, out2)`. Its input is `out1`; its output `out2` is the terminal side.

The address extends Volume I without redefining it. A Volume I coordinate is `launch:landing/side.dir.out`; a Volume II coordinate is `launch:landing/dex1|dex2`, two cube vertices separated by a bar. For example `toe:toe/O.IN.O|O.OUT.S` is a toe launch, an opposite-side inward first dexterity leaving the bag crossed, an opposite-side outward second dexterity returning it, landing on the toe. Every Volume I address remains valid and unchanged; a one-dexterity movement is the degenerate case with a single vertex.

The base map is exactly the Volume I surface lattice: the same thirty fibers, each a launch-and-landing surface pair. What changed is only the fiber content.

## 3. The geometry of the two-dex universe

**The fiber grew from the cube to the six-cube.** The single dexterity's cube is the three-dimensional binary hypercube Q3 (eight vertices, three binary axes: side, direction, output side). The two-dexterity fiber is the ordered product of two such cubes, the six-dimensional hypercube **Q6, sixty-four vertices, six binary axes**: the first dexterity's three and the second dexterity's three.

The decisive geometric finding is that **the product is free, not constrained.** The handoff between the dexterities threads the carriage state (the second dexterity's input is fixed to be the first's output), but a dexterity can act on the bag in either carriage state, so no pairing is impossible. The handoff determines the intermediate; it prunes nothing. The reference fiber `toe:toe` bears this out directly: all sixty-four of its coordinates are valid terrain, with none blocked, none artifact, none uncertain. The two-dex fiber is the whole six-cube.

This answers the volume's opening question. The two dexterities are not independent in the sense that the intermediate is shared (the first's output is the second's input), but they are unconstrained in the sense that every combination is realizable as a path. The coupling is a threading of state, not a restriction of choice.

The axis inventory, stated plainly:

```
  Volume I fiber  Q3  = 3 axes:  dex.side   dex.direction   dex.out(terminal side)
  Volume II fiber Q6  = 6 axes:  dex1.side  dex1.direction  dex1.out(intermediate carriage)
                                 dex2.side  dex2.direction  dex2.out(terminal side)
```

Two of the six axes deserve names. The **intermediate carriage** (`dex1.out`) is a genuinely new free axis with no analog in Volume I: it is the state the movement passes through, chosen by the first dexterity, invisible in the landing. The **terminal side** (`dex2.out`) is the same free axis Volume I had, now carried by the second dexterity. So the terminal side stayed free, and a new free axis appeared beneath it.

## 4. Symmetry analysis

Volume I's cube carried per-axis flip symmetries, and the direction flip paired its eight named atoms. All of those survive: each of the six axes of Q6 has its flip involution, three acting on each dexterity.

**The new symmetry, absent at one dexterity, is the dex-swap:** exchange the first dexterity's block with the second's, `(side1,dir1,out1) <-> (side2,dir2,out2)`. It is an order-two automorphism of the six-cube, and it exchanges the two Q3 factors. Nothing at one dexterity has anything to swap; this symmetry exists only because there are now two dexterities to order.

On the reference six-cube the swap partitions the sixty-four coordinates exactly:

- **The diagonal: eight fixed points.** These are the coordinates where the two dexterities are identical (`dex1 = dex2`): the eight **repeated-dexterity** movements, a dexterity performed twice. The swap leaves each unmoved.
- **The off-diagonal: fifty-six points in twenty-eight swap-orbits of two.** Each orbit is the two orderings of a distinct dexterity pair: doing dexterity A then B, and doing B then A.

The swap is a symmetry of the coordinate space and a nontrivial operation on movements: swapping an off-diagonal coordinate generally yields a different movement, because the order differs and the intermediate carriage differs (it becomes the other dexterity's output). Whether the two orderings of a given pair are ever the same movement, that is, whether two dexterities ever genuinely commute, is a movement question the atlas marks by the swap-orbit structure and does not resolve. The atlas records that the orderings are distinct coordinates and that the swap relates them; it does not claim they are or are not the same physical movement.

## 5. Structural constraints

Four structural features organize the fiber. The first is the headline of this volume; the next three lift the Volume I rules verbatim to the pair level.

- **The internal handoff (a threading, not a pruning).** The second dexterity's input is the first's output. This is a real structural fact, and it was anticipated to reduce the space below the full product. It does not: because a dexterity acts on any carriage state, the handoff is always satisfiable, and the fiber is the whole six-cube. This is the constraint that turned out to be vacuous, and it is the most important structural result in the volume.

- **Launch foreclosure, at the first dexterity (blocked).** A cross-body launch forecloses its same-side face, exactly as in Volume I, and the foreclosure falls on the launch-adjacent first dexterity: a clipper launch permits only an opposite-side first dexterity. The second dexterity, not launch-adjacent, stays free.

- **Midline collapse, at the terminal side (artifact).** A midline landing has one central target, so the terminal side has no same-versus-opposite distinction, and the second dexterity's opposite-side output is a duplicate of its same-side output. Half of each midline fiber's coordinates are artifact. Only the final landing collapses; the intermediate carriage is mid-move and stays meaningful.

- **The uncertain surface (uncertain).** A peak landing is unresolved as a surface, so every peak fiber is uncertain, unchanged from Volume I.

Beyond these, the fiber carries the diagonal-versus-off-diagonal structure of Section 4 (repeated dexterities and ordered pairs), which is geometry, not constraint.

## 8. Comparison with Volume I

**What remained the same.** The base map (the surface lattice, thirty fibers). The fiber-bundle architecture (a base of surfaces, a fiber of dexterity structure). The classification taxonomy (valid, blocked, artifact, uncertain). The addressing convention (extended by a bar, never redefined). The three lifted rules (launch foreclosure, midline collapse, peak uncertainty), which applied verbatim at the pair level. The shape of the occupancy coastline.

**What fundamentally changed.** The fiber. Volume I's fiber was the three-cube; Volume II's is the six-cube. The unit of dexterity structure doubled in dimension, and with it the movement carries an intermediate state it did not have before.

**Did new dimensions appear.** Yes, three, and two of them matter structurally. The second dexterity's three axes are new. Among them, the intermediate carriage (the first dexterity's output side) is a free axis with no Volume I analog: it is the state the movement passes through, chosen and then hidden by the landing. Order also became real: the fiber is an ordered pair, and doing A then B is a different coordinate from doing B then A.

**Did the geometry itself change.** Yes. The cube became a hypercube. The "one cube over every fiber" intuition of Volume I is not literally true at two dexterities; the correct statement is "one hypercube over every fiber," and the cube was the one-dexterity special case of it. The fiber-bundle intuition held; the cube intuition generalized rather than survived.

**Did any Volume I intuition fail.** One did, and instructively. The architecture blueprint anticipated that the two-dex space would be a constrained subset of the product, pruned by the internal handoff. That was wrong: the handoff threads the carriage but constrains nothing, and the fiber is the full free six-cube. The lesson is that a within-move transition is not a within-move restriction, and it is worth carrying into every higher volume: adding a dexterity multiplies the fiber, and the join between dexterities is a thread, not a gate.

## 9. Known boundaries and the final note

Where this volume stops is stated in `VOLUME_II_BOUNDARIES.md`: three-and-higher dexterity, the parameter and body overlay, composition between movements, and physical realizability, plus the uncertain regions this volume marked and did not resolve (the simultaneity operator, the parameter grades, and the primitive floor). The simultaneity overlay deserves one line here because it is new at two dexterities: every sequential two-dex coordinate has a simultaneous reading (the two dexterities performed together rather than in order), whose scoring rule is unresolved in the frozen grammar, so it is marked as an uncertain overlay across all 1216 valid coordinates and is not enumerated as terrain.

The structural result of Volume II, in one sentence: two-dexterity movement is a surface-fiber bundle whose fiber is the free six-cube, an ordered pair of dexterity cubes threaded by a carriage state, carrying a new dex-swap symmetry with an eight-point repeated-dexterity diagonal, and this hypercube-per-fiber picture is the object every later volume will extend.
