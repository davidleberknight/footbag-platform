# The Atlas of the Freestyle Movement Universe, Volume I

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. No production code, no dictionary rows, no authoring, no parser or generator redesign. This atlas maps the movement universe generated by Grammar v1 exactly as frozen; it does not modify the grammar, invent primitives, resolve open doctrine, or extend into higher dexterity.

Generated by `generate_atlas.py` from the frozen grammar; the machine-readable census is `atlas_catalog.csv`. The reader's guide is `ATLAS_GUIDE.md`; the narrative reading of the terrain is `LANDSCAPE_ANALYSIS.md`; where the map deliberately ends is `KNOWN_BOUNDARIES.md`.

This is a map, not a trick list. Every location is one movement coordinate. Some regions are densely named, some are empty, some are grammar structure rather than movement, and some are an unresolved frontier. The atlas records those facts; it does not explain, repair, or recommend.

## 1. What the atlas is a map of

The movement universe generated by Grammar v1, within the frozen 0-and-1-dexterity bound, is **263 coordinates**. They divide into two regions:

- **The ground floor (0-DEX): 23 coordinates.** Movements with no dexterity: held stalls on each surface, bare body steps, and bare-surface kicks.
- **The fiber bundle (1-DEX): 240 coordinates.** Movements with exactly one dexterity, organized by the structure in Section 2.

The atlas does not organize by trick name. It organizes by movement structure, because the movement language has a structure and the names do not follow it.

## 2. The coordinate system (the atlas's contribution)

The single most useful thing the atlas provides is a **coordinate system**: a stable, name-free address for every movement, derived from the grammar rather than from history.

The universe factors as a **surface-fiber bundle**:

- **The base map is the surface lattice.** A base point is a pair of surfaces: the launch surface (where the bag starts) and the landing surface (where it comes to rest). Written `launch:landing`, for example `toe:toe`, `clip:clipper`, `toe:heel`.
- **Over every base point sits the same eight-vertex dex cube.** The one dexterity has three binary choices: which side the leg circles on (`S` same-side, `O` opposite-side), which direction it circles (`IN`, `OUT`), and which side the bag lands on relative to the reference foot (`S`, `O`). A vertex is written `side.direction.termside`, for example `S.IN.S` or `O.OUT.O`.
- **A movement coordinate is a fiber plus a vertex**, written `launch:landing/side.direction.termside`. For example `toe:toe/O.IN.O` is the movement the dictionary calls mirage.

Names are a **section over this bundle**: a choice, at some base points, to label some vertices. The atlas's central finding is how thin that section is (Section 6).

The ground floor uses the same address form with the dex slot replaced by the terminal it carries: `set:toe/DEL` is a toe stall, `set:-/BOD:spin` is a spin, `set:sole/KICK` is a sole kick.

## 3. The reference cube

Exactly one fiber is fully named: `toe:toe`, the toe-launch, toe-landing fiber. Its eight vertices are the eight foundational atoms, and they populate the cube with no gaps. This is the atlas's reference cube, the one place where the movement language and the names coincide completely.

Reading the cube as two squares, one per landing side:

```
   landing side = SAME (bag returns to the reference foot)
                 dex side
              SAME            OP
      IN   around_the_world  pickup
      OUT  orbit             legover

   landing side = OP (bag crosses to the other foot)
                 dex side
              SAME     OP
      IN   pixie      mirage
      OUT  fairy      illusion
```

Every other movement in the 1-dexterity universe is the same cube sitting over a different surface pair. That is the whole architecture of the region: one cube, moved across a lattice of surfaces.

## 4. The census

The 263 coordinates, by what the grammar and the dictionary say about each:

| Status | Count | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Named (existing) | 22 | a live dictionary trick sits here |
| Named under a folk name (existing-another-name) | 3 | occupied under an opaque community name, not its structural description |
| Unnamed | 144 | grammar-valid, structurally sound, no label |
| Grammar-blocked | 40 | the grammar correctly refuses this coordinate |
| Grammar-artifact | 32 | the enumerator over-generates a duplicate the grammar does not distinguish |
| Grammar-uncertain | 22 | the coordinate depends on an unresolved grammar question |

Reading the census as terrain:

- **Movement terrain (grammar-valid): 169 coordinates.** Of these, **25 are named** (22 by structural name, 3 by folk name) and **144 are unnamed**. This is the real surface of the movement universe: the places a mover can actually go under the current grammar. Roughly **one in seven is labeled**.
- **Grammar structure, not movement: 72 coordinates.** 40 blocked (a cross-body launch reaching for its own foreclosed face) and 32 artifact (the opposite-side copy of a midline landing, which has only one central target). These are features of the map's projection, not places on the ground.
- **The uncertain frontier: 22 coordinates.** The peak surface (unresolved as a landing versus a timing marker) and the bare-surface kicks (which score a point with no scored generator). These sit at the edges the grammar has not settled.

Dropping the 32 artifacts, the atlas maps **231 distinct coordinates**.

## 5. Region tour

### 5.1 The ground floor (0-DEX, 23 coordinates)

The floor beneath the cube. Thirteen held stalls, one per landing surface (nine named: toe, inside, outside, knee, neck, head, forehead, shoulder, clipper stalls; four unnamed on the harder surfaces). Five bare body steps (spin, flying inside, flying outside named; duck and dive unnamed as bare tricks though live as operators). Five bare-surface kicks, all on the uncertain frontier because their scored point has no source in the four-generator basis. The floor is where the map is best-labeled relative to its size, because these are the oldest and most basic positions.

### 5.2 The fiber bundle (1-DEX, 240 coordinates)

The main body of the map: the eight-vertex cube over every reachable surface pair. Thirty-one fibers exist, grouped by launch class and landing grade into nine movement families (Section 7). The reference cube `toe:toe` is fully named; every other fiber is mostly or entirely unnamed, with three folk names scattered off the reference fiber.

## 6. The occupancy map

The heart of the atlas: for each fiber, how its eight vertices are occupied. `named` counts structural and folk names together.

```
  fiber (launch:landing)     named  unnamed  blocked  uncertain  artifact
  toe:toe                      8       0        0         0          0     <- the reference cube
  toe:inside                   2       6        0         0          0
  inside:inside                1       7        0         0          0
  outside:outside              1       7        0         0          0
  clipper:toe                  1       3        0         0          0
  toe:clipper                  0       8        0         0          0
  toe:heel   toe:sole          0       8        0         0          0
  toe:knee   toe:outside       0       8        0         0          0
  toe:shoulder                 0       8        0         0          0
  knee:knee  shoulder:shoulder 0       8        0         0          0
  toe:head   toe:forehead      0       4        0         0          4     <- midline: half the cube is artifact
  toe:neck   toe:cloud         0       4        0         0          4
  toe:peak                     0       0        0         8          0     <- uncertain surface
  clipper:inside  ...(paired)  0       4        4         0          0     <- cross-body launch: half blocked
  clipper:head ...(midline)    0       2        2         0          4
  clipper:peak                 0       0        0         8          0
```

Two structural facts jump out of the occupancy map:

- **Names collapse onto one fiber.** The reference cube `toe:toe` holds every one of its eight names; the entire rest of the 240-coordinate bundle holds three folk names and two structural names between all thirty other fibers. The named universe is not spread across the terrain; it is a single fully-lit fiber in an otherwise dark bundle.
- **The grammar-structural regions are systematic, not scattered.** Every clipper-launch fiber loses exactly half its cube to blocking (the same-side face the cross-body launch forecloses). Every midline landing loses exactly half its cube to artifact (the opposite-side copy of a central target). Every peak fiber is entirely uncertain. These are not noise; they are the shape of the grammar showing through the map.

```
   1/40  (  2%)  clipper-launch / plain-paired cube
   0/20  (  0%)  toe-launch / plain-midline square
   0/20  (  0%)  toe-launch / unusual-surface cube
   0/20  (  0%)  clipper-launch / plain-midline square
   0/20  (  0%)  clipper-launch / unusual-surface cube
   0/8   (  0%)  toe-launch / cross-body cube
   0/8   (  0%)  clipper-launch / cross-body cube
```

The naming is not evenly distributed. It concentrates almost entirely in one family, the toe-launch plain-paired cube, and within that family almost entirely on one fiber. Six of the nine families have no names at all. The map has one settled continent and a wide unnamed ocean.

## 8. Symmetry

The reference cube is not eight independent atoms; it is one shape with a symmetry group acting on it. Flipping the circling direction (IN against OUT) pairs the cube exactly:

```
  around_the_world  <->  orbit
  pixie             <->  fairy
  mirage            <->  illusion
  pickup            <->  legover
```

The full group is the Klein four-group generated by flipping any of the three binary axes (dex side, direction, landing side). The eight atoms are one orbit under that group. This is a genuine regularity of the movement language, not an accident of naming: the names that exist respect the symmetry (each pair above is a recognized direction-reversed partner), which is why the reference cube reads as complete rather than arbitrary.

## 9. How to use this atlas

Future work should refer to a **location**, not a trick name. "The opposite-side inward vertex of the `toe:heel` fiber" is a precise, grammar-derived address that means the same thing to a doctrine question, a promotion review, a notation decision, and a future biomechanics study. The trick name (where one exists) is an annotation on that address, not the address itself. The reader's guide explains the addressing in full.

## 10. Final question

**Has the movement language become understandable enough that future doctrine, promotion, and biomechanics work can refer to locations in the atlas instead of reasoning from isolated trick names?**

Answered only from the evidence the atlas produced, not aspirationally: **yes, for the mapped universe, and the atlas shows exactly why.**

The evidence is three structural facts the map makes unavoidable. First, the universe has a **single coordinate system** that addresses every movement without reference to any name: the surface-fiber-plus-cube-vertex address covers all 263 coordinates, and every existing trick name lands on exactly one address. Second, the naming layer is **thin and lopsided** (25 of 231 distinct coordinates named, with 8 of them on one fiber and 6 of 9 families entirely unnamed), which means name-based reasoning is reasoning from a heavily biased sample of the terrain, while address-based reasoning sees the whole surface, named or not. Third, the terrain has **exploitable regularity** (the fiber bundle structure, the Klein-four symmetry, the systematic blocked and artifact regions), so a location carries structural information a name does not: from an address alone you know the movement's family, its symmetry partners, its grade, and whether it is real terrain or grammar structure.

The honest bound on that yes: it holds for the 0-and-1-dexterity universe this atlas maps, and only there. It does not yet extend to higher dexterity, to composition, or to physical realizability, which are outside the map (see `KNOWN_BOUNDARIES.md`). Within its stated bound, the atlas is a usable common coordinate system, and the case for future branches referring to locations rather than names rests on the map itself: the names cover one seventh of the terrain and cluster on one fiber, so any question asked in names is asked about a fraction of the universe, while the same question asked in coordinates is asked about all of it.
