# Landscape Analysis: what the Movement Atlas reveals

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. A narrative reading of the terrain mapped in `ATLAS_VOLUME_I.md` and `atlas_catalog.csv`. It describes the landscape; it recommends no action, invents no names, and does not judge physical realizability. Every figure comes from the generated census.

## The shape of the country

The movement universe under Grammar v1 is a country with one settled continent and a wide unnamed ocean. The settled continent is a single fiber, the toe-launch toe-landing cube, where all eight foundational atoms sit with no gaps. Everything else, 223 of the 231 distinct coordinates, is either unnamed terrain, a handful of folk-named outposts, or the structural coastline the grammar draws. The country is small enough to hold in view and regular enough to navigate, which is the precondition for treating it as a map at all.

## Which regions are densely occupied

Only one. The toe-launch plain-paired family carries ten of the twenty-five names in the universe, and within it the reference fiber alone carries eight. Naming density falls off a cliff outside it: the self-fiber plain-paired family is at six percent, the clipper-launch plain-paired family at two percent, and the remaining six families at zero. Density is not a gradient across the map; it is a single bright spot with darkness around it. The reason is historical rather than structural: the toe stall is the oldest and most central anchor, so the movements that begin and end there were named first and named completely, while the same cube over every other surface pair was never given labels.

## Which regions are almost empty

Most of them. Six of the nine movement families have no names at all: both midline families, both unusual-surface families, and both cross-body families. These are not small: the unusual-surface and midline families alone account for eighty coordinates. They are ordinary movements by the grammar's lights (a dexterity into a heel, a sole, a head), fully formed and fully scored, and none is labeled. The emptiness is a fact about the dictionary's history, not about the movements.

## Which regions show perfect symmetry

The reference cube. Its eight atoms form one orbit of the Klein four-group generated by flipping the three binary axes, and the direction-flip axis pairs them exactly: around-the-world with orbit, pixie with fairy, mirage with illusion, pickup with legover. The symmetry is not imposed by the atlas; the existing names already honor it, because each pair is a recognized direction-reversed partner in the sport's own vocabulary. That the names respect the symmetry is why the reference cube reads as a complete, natural object rather than an arbitrary list, and it is the strongest evidence that the cube is the right unit of structure.

## Which regions contain only historical naming

Three outposts. The folk-named coordinates, guay (the inside pickup), inspinning reverse guay (the inside pixie), and bubba (the clipper-launched illusion), are occupied under opaque community names that do not describe their structure. They are the only places off the reference fiber where a name sits at all, and each is a name that would be unrecoverable from the movement without the community's memory. They mark where naming happened by accident of culture rather than by structural completion.

## Which regions are structurally complete

Exactly one: the reference cube, complete at eight of eight vertices. No other fiber is close. A few carry one or two names (the two self-fiber around-the-worlds, the inside fiber's two folk names, the clipper-launched illusion), but every other fiber is a cube with at most a quarter of its vertices lit. Structural completeness and naming completeness coincide in precisely one place, which is why that place became the reference.

## Which regions appear never to have been explored

The unusual-surface and cross-body families, and the whole clipper-launch bundle beyond its single folk name, read as untouched. Not blocked, not uncertain, just unnamed: a dexterity onto a heel or a sole or across the body into a clipper, over and over, with no label anywhere. Whether these are unexplored in movement or merely unexplored in naming is a question the atlas cannot answer and does not try to; it records that the terrain is there and the map is blank.

## Which regions are artifacts of grammar rather than movement

Seventy-two coordinates, just under a third of the raw map. Forty are blocked: every clipper-launch fiber loses its same-side face because a cross-body launch cannot reach it. Thirty-two are artifacts: every midline landing loses its opposite-side half because a central target has no same-versus-opposite distinction, so the enumerator's second copy is not a second movement. These regions are perfectly systematic, which is the tell that they belong to the grammar's projection and not to the ground. A reader who mistook them for terrain would double-count the midline surfaces and imagine movements the grammar forbids; the atlas separates them out so the real surface, the 169 valid coordinates, stands clear.

## Which regions expose unexpected regularity

Two, beyond the symmetry already noted. First, the bundle structure itself: the fact that one cube sits unchanged over every surface pair was not obvious from a trick list and is obvious from the map, and it means the entire 1-dexterity universe is generated by two small independent choices, which surface pair and which cube vertex. Second, the exactness of the grammar-structural regions: blocking removes exactly half of every clipper cube and artifact removes exactly half of every midline cube, with no exceptions across thirty-one fibers. Regularity that clean is itself information: it says the grammar's constraints are few, uniform, and global, not a patchwork of special cases.

## The landscape in one paragraph

The movement universe is a fiber bundle with a single fully-named fiber, a symmetric reference cube, a wide unnamed hinterland across six untouched families, three folk-named outposts, and a clean grammar-structural coastline of blocked and artifact regions that must not be mistaken for terrain. The named world is one seventh of the real terrain and clusters on one spot. The map is small, regular, and legible, and it makes the distribution of names look like what it is: a historical accident concentrated at the oldest anchor, not a reflection of where the movements are.
