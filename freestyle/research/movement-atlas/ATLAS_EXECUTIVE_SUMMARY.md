# The Freestyle Movement Atlas: Executive Summary

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. A plain-language summary of the completed Atlas research program (Volumes I to IV and their errata, interludes, and audits), written to close the investigation and to hand off only its durable conclusions. The Atlas is paused after Volume IV.

## The question we set out to answer

Freestyle footbag has thousands of named tricks. The research asked a simpler question underneath the names: **what is the underlying structure of freestyle movement, and how much of it is a language rather than a list?** Not how many tricks exist, and not which are hardest, but how movements are built, how they relate, and whether the space of possible movements has a natural shape.

## What we did, in four volumes

- **Volume I mapped the simplest movements.** A movement with no leg-circling, or exactly one, was given a coordinate: where the bag launches, where it lands, and the shape of the single leg-circle in between. This produced a map of the one-circle universe with a name-free address for every point, and showed that the named tricks fill only a small, lopsided corner of it: one fully-named region and a wide unnamed hinterland.

- **Volume II tried to map two-circle movements** and proposed that they were simply every pairing of two one-circle movements. That gave a clean, large map.

- **Volume II was then deliberately attacked, and the clean claim did not survive.** A hostile audit and a corpus test showed that two circles do not compose freely: the join between them carries information the notation does not fully record (which foot frames the movement, which leg is free, the state the bag passes through), and difficulty depends on the join, not just the two circles. The clean count became an upper bound, not an exact one. The interlude that followed made explicit what must pass from one circle to the next, and a test against 395 real two-circle tricks confirmed the model was incomplete and, in two respects, not recoverable from the notation at all.

- **Volume III studied the notation itself** as a way of recording movement, and measured how much of a movement survives being written down.

- **Volume IV asked how the two-circle universe is naturally organized**, deriving families from structure alone and comparing them with freestyle's historical names only afterward.

## The strongest, best-supported findings

- **Freestyle is a movement language, not just a list of named tricks.** Tricks are built from a small set of recurring movement components combined in order, and reading the components lets you read almost any trick.
- **Structure is more basic than naming.** What a trick *is* lives in its components and how they combine, not in the label. Difficulty follows the structure: the score is the count of the scored parts.
- **Names are labels on structures, and the mapping is loose.** In the live dictionary, thirty-six notation strings are each shared by two or more differently-named tricks, and many names have moved between movements over the years. One name does not guarantee one unique movement.
- **The notation is a scoring-and-identity shorthand.** It records, densely, the scored events and the structure that places a trick among its relatives, and it deliberately drops handedness and fine physical detail. It is precise about what scores and what a trick is related to, and silent about much of how a body performs it.
- **The named vocabulary covers only part of the structurally describable space.** The map contains many well-formed movements the community has never named, and the notation itself can describe forms beyond those already performed.
- **The two-circle universe is uniform, not naturally clumped.** Its geometry splits evenly on every structural distinction, so it does not fall into separated families on its own; its natural taxonomy is a set of orthogonal descriptive axes, and freestyle's historical families turn out to classify by a different axis again (how a trick lands).

## The important hypotheses we falsified

- **That two circles compose into every free pairing.** They do not; the join is constrained by information the simple model ignored, so the tidy count is only a ceiling.
- **That the notation can reconstruct a movement.** It cannot: two of the things a movement needs (which foot frames it, which leg circles) are absent from every written trick, so no trick fully reconstructs from its notation alone.
- **That the movement universe partitions into natural families.** Its geometry is uniform; the groupings that exist are imposed by which movements are actually used and by the names players chose, not by the structure itself.

## Five words this research keeps separate

The whole program depends on not confusing five things that everyday talk runs together:

- **A movement** is what a body actually does with the bag.
- **A formula** is the structural recipe: the components and their order.
- **The notation** is the written shorthand for the scored and identifying parts of that recipe, not the whole movement.
- **A name** is a label players attach to a movement to talk about it.
- **A dictionary object** is one row in the encyclopedia: a canonical name, its structure, its score, and its alternate names.

These are four different layers plus the record that ties them together, and most of the research's value is in keeping them apart: a name is not a movement, a notation is not a full movement, and a dictionary row is not the limit of what is possible.

## What the Atlas does not claim

The Atlas maps movements the grammar can *describe*, not movements a body can *perform*. Grammar-describable is not the same as physically possible, and the Atlas never claims to enumerate every performable trick. It is a map of a language, not a catalog of everything a player could ever do; the outer edge of what players may discover is deliberately left open.

## Present boundaries

The Atlas covers movements with zero, one, or two leg-circles, and studies how the notation represents them and how they organize. It does not cover three-or-more-circle movements, how one whole trick follows another (combos), the physical performability of any movement, or the unresolved questions at the foundation of the grammar (whether one basic building block is still missing, how a few modifiers should score). Those are named as edges, not crossed.

## Why it is paused now

The research reached a natural resting point: the one- and two-circle universe is mapped, the notation's role is understood, the taxonomy is described, and the durable conclusions are stable and repeatedly cross-checked. Going further would need one of three things the research cannot supply from inside itself: a ruling on the grammar's open foundation before larger generation is sound, evidence from outside the notation for the movement information the notation does not record, or a separately approved new research phase. Until one of those arrives, the honest and useful move is to stop mapping and start applying: turn the settled conclusions into better public education and cleaner production quality checks. That application is the subject of the accompanying handoff; the mapping itself is complete for now.
