# Toe-to-Toe Triple-Dex Variation Matrix: Research Note

Status: exploratory research under `exploration/`. Not production data, not doctrine, not a change to the live dictionary. A narrow, bounded application of the completed Freestyle Movement Atlas as a practical tool, before the Atlas is parked. It modifies no production code, dictionary row, alias, status, notation, or Atlas volume; it authors no trick, invents no name, recommends no promotion, and makes no physical-realizability claim. Generated by `generate_matrix.py` (read-only against the dictionary notation). Data: `triple_dex_matrix.csv`, `distinct_formula_catalog.csv`. Reports: `POSITIONAL_COLLAPSE_REPORT.md`, `OCCUPANCY_SUMMARY.md`, `FOCUSED_FINDINGS.md`.

## The question

For movements that begin from a toe stall, contain exactly three dexterity events using established dex structures only, and end on a toe stall: what structurally distinct formulas exist, and for each, which of the SAME / OP / NEAR / FAR positional readings is a genuine movement distinction versus merely redundant wording?

The motivation is concrete. Two submissions, Pixie Same-Side Eggbeater and Pixie Same-Side Double Leg Over, turned out not to be structurally distinct from their ordinary named versions. Resolving those false distinctions raised the useful question the Atlas can answer directly: if the terminal-side relationship collapses in one direction (the same-side reading adds nothing), what corresponding FAR-side variants should exist structurally?

## The bounded grammar

Included: a toe launch; exactly three dexterity events; a toe terminal delay; each dexterity an established token of leg-side (SAME or OP) crossed with direction (IN or OUT); sequential and already-established co-articulated chains; repeated and mixed dex chains; positional terminal readings.

Excluded, and never generated: body operators, spins, ducks, dives, weaving, zulu, symposium, paradox, x-dex, pogo, and unresolved operators; unusual launch and landing surfaces; kicks; non-toe endings; and any dex direction beyond IN and OUT. Where an existing named triple-dex trick needs one of these to be read (a backside token, a spinning or symposium dex, a clipper or generic set launch), it is reported as outside this bounded matrix, never silently folded in.

## Two positional axes, kept apart

The central modeling decision, and the reason the Pixie cases resolve cleanly, is that "same-side" can mean two different things, and the Atlas keeps them separate:

- **The leg-side axis.** Each dexterity's own leg relationship, SAME or OP. A difference on this axis is a different movement: it changes a dex token, so it is a different structural base. The matrix enumerates all of these as bases, never as positional readings. (The dictionary already shows this: Pixie Double Pickup and Pixie Double Pickup Same Side differ in their second dexterity's side, so they are two genuinely different tricks, two different bases.)

- **The terminal-receiver axis.** Which foot the final toe delay lands on: the near (same) foot or the far (opposite) foot. This is the positional reading under test. The four submitted labels map onto the two terminals the notation can write: SAME and NEAR both denote the near foot (`SAME TOE [DEL]`); OP and FAR both denote the far foot (`OP TOE [DEL]`).

The notation preserves the near-versus-far terminal as its same/op token, and nothing finer. So any receiver-distance nuance that NEAR or FAR might carry beyond "which foot" is not written, and a near/far reading that claims such a nuance is representation-ambiguous. This is the Atlas Volume III result applied: the notation records the scored and family-defining structure, and drops handedness and fine receiver detail.

## How the formulas were generated

- **Stage 1, bases.** Every ordered chain of three dexterities, each a (side, direction) token, from toe to toe: 4 tokens cubed, 64 leg-side bases. No names attached.

- **Stage 2, positional readings.** For each base, the four terminal-receiver labels SAME, OP, NEAR, FAR: 256 generated readings.

- **Stage 3, normalization.** The four labels collapse onto the two representable terminals (SAME and NEAR to the near terminal; OP and FAR to the far terminal), and co-articulation (a `>>` versus `>` between dexes) is folded into formula identity rather than treated as a distinct formula. Each base yields two representable formulas, one per terminal: 128 distinct formulas. The submitted wording of each reading is kept as an annotation so the reason for every collapse stays visible.

- **Stage 4, corpus comparison.** Each distinct formula is compared to the active dictionary by formula identity, never by literal name: a formula matches a trick when their dex-token sequence and terminal side agree, regardless of name or co-articulation marker.

## What the positional labels do

- **SAME versus OP terminal is a real, representable distinction.** They write different notation (`SAME TOE` versus `OP TOE`) and land on different feet. Both occur in the corpus; most named triple-dex toe-to-toe tricks land near (SAME), a few land far (OP).

- **NEAR is redundant wording for the SAME terminal, and FAR for the OP terminal.** Near means the near (same) foot and far means the far (opposite) foot, which are exactly the two terminal tokens the notation already writes. So a NEAR or FAR label adds no representable distinction; it collapses onto its same/op twin, and any receiver-distance beyond the foot it names is not preserved.

- **A leg-side "same-side" that flips a dex is not a positional reading at all.** It defines a different base, and that base is separately enumerated.

## What the two named examples demonstrate

Both submissions resolve as redundant terminal wording, and the Atlas says exactly why.

Pixie Eggbeater is the formula `TOE > SAME IN [DEX] > OP OUT [DEX] > OP OUT [DEX] > SAME TOE [DEL]`, and Pixie Double Leg Over is `TOE > SAME IN [DEX] > OP IN [DEX] > OP OUT [DEX] > SAME TOE [DEL]`. Both already land on the near (SAME) foot. A "same-side" submitted on top of a trick that already lands near is a redundant terminal label: it names the terminal the trick already has, so it adds no distinct formula and collapses onto the ordinary version. That is the whole content of the false distinction.

Crucially, the alternative reading fails, which is what confirms the terminal-wording interpretation. If "same-side" had instead been a leg-side assertion flipping the eggbeater's two OP dexes to SAME, it would have defined a different base (`SAME IN, SAME OUT, SAME OUT`), a distinct unnamed formula, which would not have collapsed. It did collapse, so the submission was a redundant terminal label, not a leg-side flip. The Atlas distinguishes the two axes and shows only one of them explains the observed collapse.

## Do corresponding FAR variants appear as distinct coordinates

Yes, and this is the useful product of resolving the false distinction. For each of the two named bases, the far (OP terminal) counterpart is a genuinely distinct, representable formula that no active trick occupies:

- `TOE > SAME IN [DEX] > OP OUT [DEX] > OP OUT [DEX] > OP TOE [DEL]` (the far counterpart of Pixie Eggbeater): distinct, unnamed.
- `TOE > SAME IN [DEX] > OP IN [DEX] > OP OUT [DEX] > OP TOE [DEL]` (the far counterpart of Pixie Double Leg Over): distinct, unnamed.

These are class D coordinates: expressible in the current notation and structurally distinct from the near forms, with no active object at their identity. This says nothing about whether a body can perform them and proposes no name; it records that the coordinate exists and is empty. The pattern generalizes across the matrix, and the occupancy summary and focused findings quantify it: the dictionary consistently names the near terminal and leaves the far terminal an empty but representable coordinate.
